**Apostle** (53 AD)

[St. Barnabas](/glossary/names#barnabas), a Levite from Cyprus originally named [Joseph](/glossary/names#joseph), received from the apostles the name Barnabas, meaning "Son of Encouragement" (Acts 4:36). An early disciple of Christ and a trusted member of the Church at Jerusalem, he introduced the newly converted [St. Paul](/people/saints/apostles/paul) to the apostles (Acts 9:27) and was later sent to Antioch, where he rejoiced at the growth of the Gospel among the Gentiles and brought Paul from Tarsus to assist in the work (Acts 11:22–26). Together they carried relief to the Christians in Judea (Acts 11:29–30), undertook missionary journeys through Cyprus and Asia Minor (Acts 13–14), and played a leading role at the Council of Jerusalem in defending the freedom of Gentile converts from the obligations of the Mosaic Law (Acts 15:1–29). After a disagreement with Paul concerning [St. Mark](/people/saints/evangelists/mark), the cousin of Barnabas (Acts 15:37–39; Col. 4:10), Barnabas returned to his native Cyprus, where tradition holds that he continued preaching the Gospel and ultimately suffered martyrdom. He is remembered as a faithful apostle, missionary, and encourager of the early church.
