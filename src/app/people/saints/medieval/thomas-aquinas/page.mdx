**Priest and Doctor of the Church** (1274 AD)

[St. Thomas Aquinas](/glossary/names#thomas), born into a noble Italian family, was educated at Monte Cassino and Naples, where he joined the Dominican Order despite strong opposition from his family, who even imprisoned him in an attempt to dissuade him; during this time he deepened his learning and confirmed his vocation. Studying under Albertus Magnus, he at first appeared slow but soon revealed extraordinary intellectual brilliance, earning lasting renown as a teacher and theologian. He produced major works such as the *<Definition lang="latin" anchor="summa-contra-gentiles" text="Summa contra Gentiles" />* and the monumental *<Definition lang="latin" anchor="summa-theologiae" text="Summa Theologiae" />*, aiming to present Christian doctrine with clarity and order, and also contributed to efforts to reconcile Eastern and Western Christianity. In his later years, after a profound mystical experience that led him to cease writing, he set out for the Second Council of Lyons but fell ill and died at the Abbey of Fossanova in 1274, leaving behind a legacy of unmatched theological depth and holiness.
