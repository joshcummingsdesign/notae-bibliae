**Bishop** (1109 AD)

[St. Anselm of Canterbury](/glossary/names#anselm), born at Aosta around 1033, entered the monastery of Bec in Normandy under the influence of Lanfranc of Pavia, eventually becoming prior and later abbot. Renowned for both holiness and intellect, he composed influential theological works such as the *<Definition lang="latin" anchor="monologium" text="Monologium" />* and *<Definition lang="latin" anchor="proslogium" text="Proslogion" />*, in which he sought to demonstrate through reason the existence and nature of God. After the death of Lanfranc, he was reluctantly appointed Archbishop of Canterbury by William II of England, and soon became embroiled in conflicts with the English crown over the authority of the Church and the recognition of the pope. Opposing royal interference in ecclesiastical affairs with firmness and humility, Anselm endured exile and political struggle before ultimately securing a settlement with Henry I of England that limited lay investiture in England. Revered for his sanctity, learning, meekness, and courage, he died peacefully at Canterbury in 1109, leaving behind a lasting legacy as one of the greatest theologians and churchmen of the medieval West.
