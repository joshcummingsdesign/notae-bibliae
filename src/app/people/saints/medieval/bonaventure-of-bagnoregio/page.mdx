**Bishop** (1274 AD)

[St. Bonaventure](/glossary/names#bonaventure) was born at Bagnoregio in Italy around 1221 and entered the Franciscan Order after a childhood illness from which he believed he had been saved through the prayers of [St. Francis of Assisi](/people/saints/medieval/francis-of-assisi). Educated at the University of Paris, he became one of the leading scholars of his age, receiving the degree of master alongside [St. Thomas Aquinas](/people/saints/medieval/thomas-aquinas) after successfully defending the [mendicant orders](/glossary/historical-terms#mendicant-orders) against their critics.

Elected Minister General of the Franciscans in 1257, Bonaventure guided the order through a period of controversy and growth, steering it along a moderate path that united intellectual rigor with the spiritual ideals of St. Francis. Under his leadership, the Franciscans became one of the most influential religious orders in the Church.

His theology sought to harmonize faith and reason, teaching that knowledge begins in faith, is developed through rational understanding, and reaches its perfection in contemplation and union with God. Deeply influenced by [St. Augustine](/people/saints/latin-fathers/augustine-of-hippo) and Pseudo-Dionysius, he emphasized prayer, virtue, and divine illumination as the means by which the soul ascends to God. His many writings, including *The Mind’s Road to God*, became classics of medieval spirituality and theology.

Later created Cardinal Bishop of Albano, Bonaventure assisted in securing the election of Pope Gregory X and was summoned to the Second Council of Lyon, where he took part in efforts to reunite the Eastern and Western Churches before his death in 1274. He was remembered as one of the greatest theologians and spiritual masters of the Middle Ages.
