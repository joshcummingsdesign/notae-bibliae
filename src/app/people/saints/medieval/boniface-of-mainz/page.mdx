**Bishop and Martyr** (755 AD)

[St. Boniface of Mainz](/glossary/names#boniface), born Winfrid in Wessex around 680 AD, left behind the prospect of honor in his native England to devote himself to the conversion of the Germanic peoples. After receiving the blessing of Pope Gregory II, he labored in Frisia, Thuringia, Hesse, and other regions of Germany, preaching in the local tongue, founding monasteries, reforming churches, and winning thousands to the Christian faith. Consecrated a bishop and later entrusted with broad authority over the German mission field, he became famous for boldly confronting paganism, most notably by felling the sacred Oak of Thor at Geismar and building a chapel from its wood. Over decades of tireless labor he organized the Church in Germany, gathered many fellow missionaries from the Anglo-Saxon monasteries, and saw vast numbers embrace Christianity. In his seventy-fifth year he returned to Frisia for one final missionary journey, where he and his companions were attacked by a pagan band near Dokkum; refusing all resistance and exhorting his followers to embrace martyrdom, Boniface met death with his head upon a volume of the Gospels on June 5, 755, sealing his lifelong witness with his blood.
