**Priest** (749 AD)

[St. John of Damascus](/glossary/names#john) was born into a prominent Christian family in Damascus during the rule of the Umayyad Caliphate. After leaving public life, he became a monk at the monastery of Mar Saba near Jerusalem and was later ordained a priest. Renowned for his learning and eloquence, he earned the title *<Definition lang="greek" anchor="khrusorrhoas" text="Chrysorroas" />*, or “golden voice,” and became known for his writings on theology, philosophy, and sacred hymnody.

John is especially remembered for his vigorous defense of the veneration of holy images during the iconoclastic controversy of the eighth century. When Emperor Leo III issued decrees against icons, John responded with a series of influential apologetic works defending their proper use in Christian worship. Written in a clear and accessible style, these treatises shaped popular opinion and later played a decisive role in the restoration of icons at the Second Council of Nicaea.

According to tradition, Leo III sought to discredit John by forging letters implicating him in treason against the Muslim rulers of Damascus, resulting in the severing of John’s right hand. After fervent prayer before an icon of the Virgin Mary, his hand was said to have been miraculously restored. In thanksgiving, he attached a silver hand to the icon, later known as the *<Definition lang="greek" anchor="tricherousa" text="Tricherousa" />*, which remains venerated in the monastery of Hilandar on Mount Athos.

Beyond the icon controversy, John made enduring contributions to Christian theology, especially through his articulation of *<Definition lang="greek" anchor="perichoresis" text="perichoresis" />*, describing both the union of Christ’s two natures and the relationship of the persons of the Trinity. Though condemned during his lifetime by the iconoclastic Council of Hieria, his writings were later vindicated and came to hold lasting authority in both Eastern and Western Christianity.
