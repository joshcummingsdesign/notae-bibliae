**Bishop** (375 AD)

[St. Athanasius of Alexandria](/glossary/names#athanasius) was born into a Christian family in Alexandria and, from an early age, displayed remarkable promise, prompting Bishop Alexander of Alexandria to take him under his care and prepare him for the ministry. Ordained a deacon in 319 AD, Athanasius became closely associated with the Desert Fathers, especially [St. Anthony the Great](/people/saints/desert-fathers/anthony-the-great), whose life he later recorded. His early theological writings, particularly *Against the Heathen* and *On the Incarnation*, established him as one of the foremost Christian thinkers of his generation.

As Alexander's assistant, Athanasius became deeply involved in the controversy surrounding Arius, who denied the full divinity of Christ. He attended the First Council of Nicaea in 325 AD, where he vigorously defended the doctrine that the Son is of one essence with the Father, becoming one of the foremost champions of the Nicene faith. Following Alexander's death, Athanasius was elected Bishop of Alexandria in 326 AD despite his reluctance to assume the office during such turbulent times.

For nearly five decades Athanasius shepherded the churches of Egypt and Libya while enduring relentless opposition from the Arians and their political allies. Over the course of his episcopate he spent more than seventeen years in exile under four Roman emperors, repeatedly fleeing for his life yet steadfastly refusing to compromise the Nicene faith. Throughout these trials he found refuge and support among the monks of the Egyptian desert, including [St. Pachomius the Great](/people/saints/desert-fathers/pachomius-the-great).

Athanasius died peacefully in 373 AD after consecrating Peter II as his successor. Revered as a Doctor of the Church and often called the "Father of Orthodoxy," he left an enduring legacy through his theological writings, his defense of Christ's full divinity, and his unwavering commitment to the orthodox faith in the face of persecution and exile.
