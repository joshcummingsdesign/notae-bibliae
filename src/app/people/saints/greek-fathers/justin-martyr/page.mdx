**Martyr** (165 AD)

[St. Justin Martyr](/glossary/names#justin) was born around 100 AD at Flavia Neapolis in Samaria, near the ancient biblical city of Shechem, and spent his youth searching for truth among the philosophical schools of his day. Unsatisfied by the Stoics, Aristotelians, and Pythagoreans, he eventually embraced Platonism before encountering an elderly Christian who persuaded him that the prophets and the revelation of Christ offered a surer path to the knowledge of God than human philosophy.

Convinced by the truth of Christianity and inspired by the courage of the martyrs, Justin converted and devoted himself to proclaiming what he called the “true philosophy.” Adopting the dress and manner of a philosopher, he traveled widely teaching the faith and eventually settled at Rome, where he established a school and instructed pupils such as Tatian.

Justin became one of the earliest and most important Christian apologists. In his surviving *Apologies* and *Dialogue with Trypho*, he defended Christians against false accusations, argued for the reasonableness of the faith, and maintained that the divine Logos had been at work throughout history, preparing even pagan philosophers such as Socrates and Plato for the coming of Christ.

During the reign of Marcus Aurelius, Justin was denounced to the authorities and brought before the prefect Junius Rusticus. Refusing to sacrifice to the Roman gods, he and several companions boldly confessed Christ, declaring that they desired to suffer for their Lord rather than deny him. Condemned according to Roman law, they were scourged and beheaded, receiving the martyrdom from which Justin derives his enduring title.
