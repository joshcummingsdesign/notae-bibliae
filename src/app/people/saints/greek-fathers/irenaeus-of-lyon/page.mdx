**Bishop and Martyr** (202 AD)

[St. Irenaeus of Lyon](/glossary/names#irenaeus) grew up in Smyrna and, according to tradition, was a disciple of [St. Polycarp](/people/saints/apostolic-fathers/polycarp-of-smyrna), who himself had been taught by [St. John the Apostle](/people/saints/apostles/john). Serving as a priest in Lyon during the persecution under Emperor Marcus Aurelius, he was sent to Rome on behalf of the Church before returning to succeed the martyred Pothinus as bishop of Lyon.

As bishop, Irenaeus devoted himself to preaching, missionary work, and defending the Christian faith. His most celebrated work, *Against Heresies*, refuted [Gnosticism](/glossary/historical-terms#gnosticism) by appealing to the authority of Scripture, the apostolic tradition, and the succession of bishops from the apostles as the sure guardians of Christian doctrine.

Irenaeus also made enduring contributions to the Church's understanding of the New Testament. He is the earliest surviving writer to affirm the authority of all four Gospels together, to identify the Gospels of John and Luke with their traditional authors, and to treat the thirteen Pauline Epistles as the authentic writings of St. Paul the Apostle. His writings quote more than twenty of the twenty-seven books of the New Testament.

Irenaeus urged Pope Victor I not to excommunicate the [Quartodeciman](/glossary/historical-terms#quartodecimanism) churches of Asia Minor over the date of Easter, demonstrating his desire to preserve the unity of the Church amid doctrinal differences. Honored as a martyr, he is remembered as one of the most important theologians of the second century and a faithful witness to the apostolic faith.
