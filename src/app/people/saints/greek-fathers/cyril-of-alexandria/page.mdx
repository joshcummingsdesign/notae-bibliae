**Bishop** (444 AD)

[St. Cyril of Alexandria](/glossary/names#cyril) was born in Egypt and educated under the guidance of his uncle, Theophilus of Alexandria, Patriarch of Alexandria. Well versed in theology and the writings of earlier Christian scholars, Cyril accompanied his uncle to the Synod of the Oak in 403 AD, where [St. John Chrysostom](/people/saints/greek-fathers/john-chrysostom) was deposed. Following Theophilus' death in 412 AD, Cyril succeeded him as Patriarch of Alexandria during a period of intense religious and political unrest.

Early in his episcopate, Cyril proved himself a steadfast defender of the Nicene faith, suppressing the [Novatianist](/glossary/historical-terms#novationism) schism and strengthening the Church in Alexandria. His leadership, however, was marked by considerable controversy amid the city's conflicts between Christians, Jews, and pagans.

Cyril is best remembered for his opposition to Nestorius, who denied that the Virgin Mary could rightly be called *<Definition lang="greek" anchor="theotokos" text="Theotokos" />* because he distinguished Christ's human and divine natures too sharply. At the Council of Ephesus in 431 AD, Cyril presided over the council that condemned Nestorius, though the proceedings were initially marred by a dispute with St. John of Antioch and the Antiochene bishops. The division was healed two years later when Cyril and John agreed to the Formula of Reunion.

A prolific theologian and biblical commentator, Cyril authored numerous works, including commentaries on Scripture, *Against the Arians*, *Dialogues on the Trinity*, and extensive writings defending the orthodox doctrine of Christ. His theological legacy profoundly shaped the Church's understanding of the Incarnation, and he is remembered as one of the great Fathers of the early Church.
