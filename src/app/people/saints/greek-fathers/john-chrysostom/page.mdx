**Bishop and Doctor of the Church** (407 AD)

[St. John Chrysostom](/glossary/names#chrysostom), born around 347 in Antioch to a military family, was raised by his devout mother Anthusa and trained in rhetoric under the pagan Libanius before dedicating himself fully to the Christian life and the study of Scripture. Though drawn to monasticism, he remained at home for a time out of filial duty, later embracing ascetic practices and eventually entering the ministry, serving as deacon and priest before becoming Archbishop of Constantinople in 398. There he vigorously reformed clerical abuses, promoted care for the poor, and inspired widespread moral renewal through his eloquence, earning both popular admiration and powerful opposition. His conflicts with Theophilus, Patriarch of Alexandria, and Empress Eudoxia led to his condemnation and repeated exiles, despite brief recalls prompted by public outcry and divine intervention. Forced into harsh journeys in exile, he died in 407 near Comana in Pontus, his final words being a doxology of praise. His life, marked by both suffering and faithfulness, was widely revered, and his legacy endured through his preaching, reforms, and enduring witness.
