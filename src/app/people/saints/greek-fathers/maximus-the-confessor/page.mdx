**Confessor** (662 AD)

[St. Maximus the Confessor](/glossary/names#maximus) was a Byzantine monk, theologian, and former civil servant who served under Emperor Heraclius. Though little is known of his early life, he was highly educated and likely born near Constantinople. He eventually left public service to embrace the monastic life at Chrysopolis, where he became abbot. Following Persian invasions, he fled to North Africa, where he came under the influence of Sophronius of Jerusalem and deepened his study of theology, especially the works of [St. Gregory of Nazianzus](/people/saints/cappadocian-fathers/gregory-of-nazianzus) and Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite.

During his time in Carthage, Maximus became a central figure in the Christological controversy over [Monothelitism](/glossary/historical-terms#monothelitism), which held that Christ had only one will. Opposing this view, Maximus defended the teaching that Christ possesses both a human and a divine will, in keeping with the earlier definitions of the Council of Chalcedon. In a public debate with Pyrrhus of Constantinople, Maximus successfully argued his position, leading Pyrrhus to temporarily renounce Monothelitism.

Maximus later traveled to Rome, where he supported Pope Martin I and participated in the Lateran Council of 649, which condemned Monothelitism. For his steadfast opposition to imperial religious policy, he was arrested under Emperor Constans II and brought to Constantinople. There he was tried, exiled, and eventually subjected to brutal punishment—his tongue cut out and his right hand severed—to silence his teaching. He was then banished to a remote region, where he died in exile in 662 AD.

Maximus’ teaching was vindicated after his death by the Third Council of Constantinople (680–681 AD), which condemned Monothelitism. Honored soon after his death, Maximus came to be recognized as one of the great theologians of the Church, revered in both the Eastern and Western traditions. His unwavering defense of orthodox Christology and his suffering for the truth earned him the title <Definition lang="english" anchor="confessor" text='"Confessor"' /> and secured his lasting place among the Fathers of the Church.
