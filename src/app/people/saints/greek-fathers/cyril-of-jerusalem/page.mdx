**Bishop and Doctor of the Church** (389 AD)

[St. Cyril of Jerusalem](/glossary/names#cyril) was a bishop of Jerusalem and later recognized as a Doctor of the Church. Born near Jerusalem, he was well-educated in both Christian theology and Greek philosophy. He was ordained deacon by Macarius of Jerusalem and later priest by Maximus of Jerusalem, whom he eventually succeeded around 350 AD. In his earlier years, he was accused of Arian sympathies, likely due to his hesitation to adopt the term *<Definition lang="greek" anchor="homoousios" text="homoousios" />* during a contentious time.

Cyril’s episcopate was marked by prolonged conflict, especially with Acacius of Caesarea, who opposed the growing prominence of Jerusalem. During a time of famine, Cyril reportedly sold valuable church items to aid the poor, which led to accusations against him and his deposition in 357 AD. Over the following years, shifting political and ecclesiastical forces led to multiple exiles and restorations, as emperors and councils alternately supported or opposed him. Despite these trials, Cyril remained closely tied to his see and repeatedly returned to Jerusalem when circumstances allowed.

By the time of the First Council of Constantinople, Cyril’s orthodoxy was firmly established, and he affirmed the use of *homoousios* to describe the relationship between the Father and the Son. His theology reflects the broader tradition of the Greek Fathers, emphasizing human free will and the healing power of God’s grace. His writings are especially notable for their warm and pastoral tone, highlighting divine mercy, forgiveness, and the gentle work of the Holy Spirit in the life of the believer.

Cyril is best known for his *Catechetical Lectures*, a series of instructions given to catechumens, along with the *Mystagogic Catecheses*, which expound the mysteries of Baptism, Confirmation, and the Eucharist. These works offer a vivid and enduring witness to the faith and practice of the early Church. Through his teaching, his perseverance in exile, and his charity toward his opponents, Cyril stands as a faithful shepherd and teacher, whose legacy continues to shape the Church’s understanding of the sacraments and the life of grace.
