**Bishop** (early 2nd century AD)

[St. Papias of Hierapolis](/glossary/names#papias) was an early Christian bishop about whom little is known beyond fragments of his own writings. He is described by [St. Irenaeus](/people/saints/greek-fathers/irenaeus-of-lyon) as a hearer of [St. John](/people/saints/apostles/john) and a companion of [St. Polycarp](/people/saints/apostolic-fathers/polycarp-of-smyrna), while [Eusebius](/people/scholars/eusebius) identifies him as Bishop of Hierapolis in Asia Minor. Papias authored a five-volume work titled *Exposition of the Sayings of the Lord*, now lost except for excerpts preserved by later writers, which remains an important witness to early Christian tradition.

Papias placed great value on oral tradition, preferring the testimony of those who had direct contact with the apostles over written sources. He carefully questioned travelers and disciples who passed through Hierapolis, seeking to learn what the apostles and their immediate followers had taught. Among those he consulted were Aristion, Bishop of Smyrna, and John the Apostle, residing in nearby Ephesus, along with others connected to the earliest Christian communities. His method reflects a desire to preserve authentic apostolic teaching as it was handed down through living memory.

Through these inquiries, Papias gathered traditions concerning the words and deeds of Christ, emphasizing fidelity to what had been received from trustworthy witnesses. He also drew from local sources, including traditions associated with the daughters of [St. Philip the Evangelist](/people/saints/biblical/philip-the-evangelist) who resided in Hierapolis. His work thus serves as a valuable link between the apostolic age and the later written accounts, preserving insights into how early Christians transmitted and understood the teachings of Jesus.

Papias is especially significant for providing some of the earliest surviving testimony about the origins of the Gospels. He records that [St. Mark](/people/saints/evangelists/mark), acting as Peter’s interpreter, accurately wrote down what he remembered of the Lord’s words and deeds, though not in strict order. He also states that [St. Matthew](/people/saints/apostles/matthew) arranged the sayings of the Lord in an ordered form and in a Hebrew style of writing. Though brief, these accounts have made Papias an important source for understanding the formation of the New Testament and the transmission of apostolic tradition.
