**Companion of St. Paul** (1st century AD)

[St. Silas](/glossary/names#silas), also known as Silvanus, was a leader and prophet in the Church at Jerusalem who, together with Judas Barsabbas, was chosen to accompany [St. Paul](/people/saints/apostles/paul) and [St. Barnabas](/people/saints/apostles/barnabas) to Antioch following the Council of Jerusalem (Acts 15:22). After Paul and Barnabas parted ways, Silas became Paul's companion on his second missionary journey, during which they were imprisoned at Philippi until a miraculous earthquake opened the prison doors and loosed their chains (Acts 16:25-37).

Together with [St. Timothy](/people/saints/biblical/timothy), Silas accompanied Paul through Macedonia, helping to establish the church at Thessalonica despite fierce opposition that forced the missionaries to flee (Acts 17:1-10). The three continued their ministry in Berea before reuniting in Corinth, where they encouraged the growing churches.

Silas is named alongside Paul in the opening of First and Second Thessalonians (1 Thess. 1:1) (2 Thess. 1:1) and is mentioned in Second Corinthians as a fellow preacher of Christ (2 Cor. 1:19). Through Timothy, he and Paul received the joyful news that the Thessalonian Christians remained steadfast in the faith despite persecution (1 Thess. 3:6).

Silas also assisted [St. Peter](/people/saints/apostles/peter) in the sending of his First Epistle, where Peter commended him as "a faithful brother" (1 Pet. 5:12). Remembered as a prophet, missionary, and faithful companion of the apostles, Silas played an important role in the spread and strengthening of the early Church.
