**The First Hermit** (341 AD)<br/>

[St. Paul of Thebes](/glossary/names#paul), known as the First Hermit, was born in the Thebaid of Egypt and orphaned at a young age. When a dispute over his inheritance arose with his brother-in-law, Paul was struck by the sight of a funeral procession and reflected on the futility of worldly wealth. Renouncing all claims to his possessions, he withdrew to a grave to pray for guidance. Soon after, learning that his brother-in-law intended to betray him during a time of persecution, Paul fled into the desert, seeking refuge and a life devoted entirely to God.

During the persecutions under Decius and Valerian, Paul settled in the Theban desert, where he embraced a life of complete solitude. What began as flight became a lifelong vocation, and he remained there for nearly a century in prayer and ascetic discipline. He lived in a cave beside a spring and a palm tree, which provided him with clothing and food. After many years, a raven miraculously brought him bread each day, sustaining him in his hidden life “alone with God alone,” a phrase later associated with his way of life.

Near the end of his life, [St. Anthony the Great](/people/saints/desert-fathers/anthony-the-great) was divinely led to seek him out in the inner wilderness. The two holy men met with mutual reverence and spoke together of God’s greatness. During their meeting, the raven brought a full loaf of bread instead of the usual portion, which Paul interpreted as a sign of God’s provision for them both. Sensing that his death was near, Paul asked Anthony to bring him the tunic of [St. Athanasius of Alexandria](/people/saints/greek-fathers/athanasius-of-alexandria).

When Anthony returned, he found that Paul had died. He clothed the body in Athanasius’ tunic and prepared for burial, at which point two lions appeared and dug Paul’s grave, an event understood as a sign of divine honor. Anthony later kept Paul’s garment as a sacred relic, wearing it only on Easter and Pentecost. Paul of Thebes came to be remembered as the first Christian hermit, whose hidden life of prayer and renunciation became a model for later monastic tradition.
