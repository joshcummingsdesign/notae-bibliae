**Martyr** (269 AD)

[St. Valentine of Rome](/glossary/names#valentine) is remembered as a Christian martyr, though the details of his life are uncertain. He is traditionally described as either a priest in Rome or the Bishop of Interamna (modern Terni in central Italy).

According to early accounts, Valentine was placed under house arrest for preaching the Gospel and brought before a judge named Asterius. When Asterius challenged him to heal his blind daughter, Valentine prayed and restored her sight. Moved by this miracle, the judge asked what he should do, and Valentine instructed him to renounce idolatry, fast, and receive baptism. Asterius obeyed, and he, along with his family and household, embraced the Christian faith and freed the prisoners under his authority.

Valentine was later arrested again and brought before Emperor Claudius II. Though the emperor initially favored him, Valentine’s attempt to convert him to Christianity led to his condemnation. Refusing to renounce his faith, Valentine was beaten and beheaded outside the Flaminian Gate on February 14, 269, sealing his witness through martyrdom.

Over time, various traditions grew up around his memory. One account holds that he wrote a farewell message to the judge’s daughter signed “from your Valentine,” while another claims he secretly performed Christian marriages in defiance of imperial orders, giving heart-shaped parchment to remind them of their vows and of God’s love. In the Middle Ages, his feast became associated with romance, especially through the influence of the 14th-century English poet Geoffrey Chaucer, who linked the day with the pairing of birds—helping to establish February 14 as a celebration of love.
