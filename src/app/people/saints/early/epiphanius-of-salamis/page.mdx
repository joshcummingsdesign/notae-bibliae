**Bishop** (403 AD)

[St. Epiphanius of Salamis](/glossary/names#epiphanius) was born into a Greek-speaking Jewish family near Hebron and converted to Christianity after witnessing the charity of a monk named Lucian. Embracing the ascetic life, he entered a monastery under the guidance of St. Hilarion (disciple of [St. Anthony the Great](/people/saints/desert-fathers/anthony-the-great)), later founded his own monastery in Palestine, and became renowned for his holiness, learning, and mastery of several languages, earning from [St. Jerome](/people/saints/latin-fathers/jerome-of-stridon) the title *<Definition lang="greek" anchor="pentaglossos" text="Pentaglossos" />*.

In 367 AD, Epiphanius was elected Bishop of Salamis in Cyprus, where he served for nearly forty years. He traveled widely preaching the Gospel, strengthening the Church, and defending the Nicene faith against heresies such as Arianism and Apollinarianism, while taking part in important synods and councils of his day.

Epiphanius became especially known for his vigorous opposition to [Origenism](/glossary/historical-terms#origenism), urging bishops to reject teachings he believed contrary to the faith. During a visit to Palestine, he famously tore down a church curtain bearing an image of Christ or a saint, believing such images to be contrary to the Scriptures. His zeal drew him into controversies involving [St. John Chrysostom](/people/saints/greek-fathers/john-chrysostom), Theophilus of Alexandria, and other leading churchmen, though, realizing he had been drawn into a political dispute against Chrysostom, he departed Constantinople and died on his journey home in 403 AD.

A prolific writer, Epiphanius is best known for the *Panarion* ("Bread Basket"), a comprehensive refutation of heresies intended to safeguard the faithful from false doctrine. His other works, including the *Ancoratus* ("The Well-Anchored Man") and *On Weights and Measures*, further established his reputation as one of the foremost defenders of Christian orthodoxy in the fourth century.
