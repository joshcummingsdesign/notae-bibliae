**Bishop and Doctor of the Church** (391 AD)

[St. Gregory of Nazianzus](/glossary/names#gregory) was one of the three Cappadocian Fathers, alongside [St. Basil the Great](/people/saints/cappadocian-fathers/basil-the-great) and [St. Gregory of Nyssa](/people/saints/cappadocian-fathers/gregory-of-nyssa). Born in Cappadocia to a Christian family, he was educated widely, including in Athens where he formed a lifelong friendship with Basil. After a formative experience at sea in which he vowed himself to Christ, Gregory was baptized and later ordained, eventually collaborating with Basil on theological works such as the *<Definition lang="greek" anchor="philokalia" text="Philocalia" />*, a compilation of writings from [Origen](/people/scholars/origen).

Returning to Nazianzus, Gregory was ordained a presbyter and became an influential defender of Christianity, especially against the pagan emperor Julian the Apostate. Through his writings, he emphasized the triumph of Christianity through patience and love, tied to the doctrine of <Definition lang="greek" anchor="theosis" text="theosis" />. He also played a major role in combating Arianism alongside Basil, engaging in theological debates that helped secure Nicene orthodoxy in Cappadocia. Though briefly made bishop of Sasima, Gregory preferred a quieter life and often withdrew from ecclesiastical ambition, choosing instead service, preaching, and ascetic living.

In 379 AD, Gregory was called to Constantinople to restore Nicene Christianity. There, preaching from a small chapel called <Definition lang="greek" anchor="anastasis" text="Anastasia" />, he delivered his famous theological orations, articulating the doctrine of the Trinity and defending the full divinity of the Holy Spirit and the full humanity of Christ. His influence grew rapidly despite opposition, including violence from Arian factions and betrayal from within. Eventually, with the support of Emperor Theodosius I, Gregory was established as Bishop of Constantinople, solidifying his role as a leading theological voice of the Church.

At the First Council of Constantinople in 381 AD, Gregory presided but soon faced challenges to his authority on canonical grounds. Rather than deepen division, he chose to resign in a spirit of humility and unity, likening himself to Jonah, willing to be cast overboard for the salvation of the ship, though innocent of the storm. After delivering a final oration, he returned to Cappadocia, where he continued briefly in ministry before retiring to a life of solitude. He died in 391 AD, remembered as a profound theologian, eloquent preacher, and steadfast defender of orthodox Christian doctrine.
