**Bishop** (379 AD)

[St. Basil the Great](/glossary/names#basil) was born at Caesarea in Cappadocia into a remarkable Christian family that included his sister St. Macrina and his brother [St. Gregory of Nyssa](/people/saints/cappadocian-fathers/gregory-of-nyssa). After receiving a distinguished education and forming a lifelong friendship with [St. Gregory of Nazianzus](/people/saints/cappadocian-fathers/gregory-of-nazianzus) at Athens, he embraced the ascetic life, studied the practices of monks in Egypt, Palestine, and Syria, and established a monastic community in Pontus whose rule became the foundation of Eastern monasticism. Ordained priest and later elected Bishop of Caesarea in 370, Basil became one of the foremost defenders of the Nicene faith against Arianism, laboring to strengthen the Church through preaching, theology, liturgical reform, charitable works, and pastoral care, including the establishment of a large complex for the poor and sick. Despite continual illness, political opposition, and ecclesiastical disputes, he remained steadfast in his ministry until his death in 379, leaving a lasting legacy as a theologian, monastic founder, and defender of the Holy Trinity. Alongside St. Gregory of Nyssa and St. Gregory of Nazianzus, he is remembered as one of the three great Cappadocian Fathers of the Church.
