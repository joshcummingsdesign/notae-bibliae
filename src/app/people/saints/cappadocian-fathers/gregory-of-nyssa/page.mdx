**Bishop** (c. 390 AD)

[St. Gregory of Nyssa](/glossary/names#gregory) was one of the three Cappadocian Fathers, alongside his brother [St. Basil the Great](/people/saints/cappadocian-fathers/basil-the-great) and his friend [St. Gregory of Nazianzus](/people/saints/cappadocian-fathers/gregory-of-nazianzus). Born in Cappadocia to a large and devout Christian family, he was raised under the influence of his mother Emmelia and his sister Macrina, both noted for their piety. His early formation combined this strong spiritual upbringing with study in classical literature and philosophy, likely in Caesarea. Though quieter and more reserved than Basil, Gregory developed a deeply reflective theological mind, drawing in part from the influence of [Origen](/people/scholars/origen) and grounding his thought in Scripture and the apostolic tradition.

As bishop of Nyssa, Gregory faced early challenges, including opposition to his leadership and disagreements even with Basil on certain pastoral approaches—particularly regarding how to treat groups like the [Sabellianists](/glossary/historical-terms#sabellianism). In 376 AD he was deposed by a local synod amid ecclesiastical tensions, though he was restored to his see two years later. Around the time of his restoration, Basil died, and Gregory assumed many of his brother’s responsibilities in the region, gradually emerging as a leading theological voice in Cappadocia.

Gregory took part in several important ecclesiastical efforts to preserve unity within the Church, including the Synod of Antioch in 379 AD, where he attempted—unsuccessfully—to reconcile divided factions amid ongoing Arian controversy. He was briefly elected Bishop of Sebaste in 380 AD but resigned due to conflict. During this period, he delivered his notable homilies on Ecclesiastes, in which he condemned the pride and injustice of slavery, arguing that no human being has rightful dominion over another, since all belong to God.[^1] He also traveled to Jerusalem in an attempt to mediate disputes surrounding [St. Cyril of Jerusalem](/people/saints/greek-fathers/cyril-of-jerusalem), though without success.

Gregory played a role in the First Council of Constantinople, contributing to the defense and clarification of Nicene orthodoxy, especially in the doctrine of the Trinity. He remained active in church affairs well into his later years. Though not known for strong administrative leadership, he proved to be a profound and influential theologian whose writings helped shape the Church’s understanding of the Trinity and the Nicene Creed. He died around 394 AD, leaving a lasting legacy as one of the Church’s great doctrinal teachers.
