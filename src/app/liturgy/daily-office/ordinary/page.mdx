<Initial text="Daily Office: Ordinary" />

The <Definition lang="latin" anchor="ordinarius" text="Ordinary" /> sets forth the fixed order of the Daily Office along with the customary manner in which it is celebrated.

## Before the Office
---

## Ceremonial

This Office commends the use of the [cassock](/liturgy/vesture/clerical-dress#cassock) and [surplice](/liturgy/vesture/vestments#surplice) as the customary vesture of the ministers.[^1] It is likewise customary that two altar candles be lighted.[^2]

## Silent Prayer

Before the Office, it is customary to kneel in silent prayer. The 15th-century [*Myroure of Our Ladye*](/meditations/silent-prayer-before-the-daily-office), written for the Bridgettine Sisters of Syon Abbey in West London, beautifully describes in detail the purpose of kneeling in silent prayer as a way of preparing one's heart and mind for the Office.[^3]

## Introductory Rites
---

## Opening Sentence

The Introductory Rites of the Office begin with the Opening Sentence.[^4] Ordinarily, the ministers proceed from the [sacristy](/glossary/liturgical-terms#sacristy) to the [chancel](/glossary/liturgical-terms#chancel), at which time all stand. The ministers take their [appointed places](/glossary/liturgical-terms#decani), and the Officiant turns to the congregation and reads the Opening Sentence proper to the day. The Opening Sentence serves as a scriptural call to worship, setting the spiritual tone for the Office.[^5] The Opening Sentences for this Office are drawn principally from the Prayer Book. Additional Opening Sentences have been drawn from the [*Saint Dunstan's Psalter*](/glossary/liturgical-terms#saint-dunstans-plainsong-psalter) (SDP) to provide a broader selection for the liturgical year.[^6]

## General Confession

After the Opening Sentence, the Officiant reads the Exhortation. At the words, "accompany me with a pure heart," the Officiant and the people kneel. Alternatively, the shorter Bidding may be read if the congregation is already familiar with the theology of the Office. In this case, all kneel after the Bidding.[^7]

Then all recite the General Confession.[^8] The General Confession is an act of repentance in which we "with confidence draw near to the throne of grace, that we may receive mercy and find grace to help in time of need" (Heb. 4:16). The 1530 [*Augsburg Confession*](/meditations/luther-on-repentance) beautifully describes in detail the meaning and purpose of repentance.[^9] Reciting the General Confession at Morning and Evening Prayer reflects the ancient practice of twice-daily confession at [Prime](/glossary/liturgical-terms#prime) and [Compline](/glossary/liturgical-terms#compline).[^10]

Note that here, as in all other instances, the *Amen* is recited by the entire congregation. Because it forms part of a text recited by all, it is not italicized. When the Prayer Book italicizes *Amen*, it signifies the congregation's response to a prayer said by the Officiant.[^11]

After the General Confession, the priest stands and faces the congregation to pronounce the Absolution.[^12] At the words, "He pardoneth and absolveth," he makes the [Sign of the Cross](/liturgy/prayers/sign-of-the-cross) toward the congregation.[^13] If a priest is not present, the Officiant remains kneeling and recites the [Collect](/glossary/liturgical-terms#collect) for the Twenty-First Sunday after Trinity in place of the Absolution.[^14]

## Our Father

If not already kneeling, the Officiant kneels with the congregation for the [Our Father](/liturgy/prayers/our-father).[^15] The Our Father serves as a liturgical response to the Absolution. As the burning coal purified Isaiah's lips, so the Absolution cleanses us and prepares us to respond, "Here I am!" (Isa. 6:6-8), to the glory of God's holy Name. Having thus been forgiven, we are able to pray with confidence, "Our Father."

It is customary to make the Sign of the Cross at the end of the Our Father.[^16] This harmonizes with the [Sarum Breviary](/liturgy/rites/sarum-rite), which states, "Before the beginning of the [Hours](/glossary/liturgical-terms#liturgy-of-the-hours), the Lord's Prayer is always said ... Whence [Jerome](/people/saints/latin-fathers/jerome-of-stridon), 'At the beginning of any work whatsoever, preface it with the Lord's Prayer and the Sign of the Cross on the forehead, according to what is written: Before prayer, prepare thy soul for the Lord; and be not as a man that tempteth God' (Ecclus. 18:23)."[^17]

[*The Myroure of Our Ladye*](/meditations/the-lords-prayer-an-exposition-of-the-our-father) wonderfully expounds the rich meaning of the Our Father.[^18]

## Office Proper
---

## Opening Versicles

The Office Proper begins with the chanting of the Opening Versicles.[^19] The 6th century [*Rule of Saint Benedict*](/meditations/the-rule-of-saint-benedict) prescribed two [versicles](/glossary/liturgical-terms#versicle) for the opening of [Matins](/glossary/liturgical-terms#matins): the [*Domine labia mea*](/liturgy/music/chants/domine-labia-mea) and the [*Deus in adjutorium*](/liturgy/music/chants/deus-in-adjutorium). This was to be followed by the [*Gloria Patri*](/liturgy/music/chants/gloria-patri) and the [*Alleluia*](/liturgy/music/chants/alleluia). The *Domine labia mea* was to be omitted the other hours of the day, and the *Alleluia* was to be omitted during certain times of the year.[^20]

The Prayer Book simplified these rubrics by appointing the *Domine labia mea* for both Morning and Evening Prayer, as it flows more naturally into the praise of the [Invitatory](/glossary/liturgical-terms#invitatory),[^21] and by replacing the *Alleluia* with its English equivalent, "Praise ye the Lord," to be used throughout the year. It also added the response, "The Lord's Name be praised," thus allowing the entire opening to be chanted <Definition lang="english" anchor="antiphonal" text="antiphonally" />.

Still kneeling, the *Domine labia mea* is recited. The Psalter directs that the Sign of the Cross be made on the lips at the *Domine labia mea*.[^22] The Roman Breviary specifies that this is done with the thumb of the right hand.[^23] Then all stand and bow toward the altar for the *Gloria Patri*. At the words, "As it was in the beginning," all rise and return to their lateral position.

It is customary to bow toward the altar whenever reciting the *Gloria Patri*, unless the appointed posture is seated.[^24] With the exception of the [*Benedicite*](/liturgy/music/chants/benedicite), which ends with its own [doxology](/glossary/liturgical-terms#doxology),[^25] this Office concludes every psalm and [canticle](/glossary/liturgical-terms#canticle) with the *Gloria Patri*,[^26] even when the Prayer Book permits an alternative.

According to ancient precedent, the Opening Versicles and the Invitatory are omitted during the last three days of Holy Week and on All Souls' Day in recognition of the solemn character of these observances.[^27]

## Invitatory

At Morning Prayer, the Invitatory follows the Opening Versicles.[^28] As the name implies, the Invitatory serves as a calling or invitation to praise God in the Daily Office. In the 6th century, the *Rule of Saint Benedict* prescribed Psalm 95, the [*Venite*](/liturgy/music/chants/venite), as the Invitatory for Matins and directed that it be sung with an [antiphon](/glossary/liturgical-terms#antiphon).[^29] The manner of recitation has varied over the centuries, but the 15th-century *Myroure of Our Ladye* explains that the antiphon is sung five times in full during the *Venite*, corresponding to the five senses, which those who serve the Lord must carefully guard.[^30] Accordingly, the Psalter marks five places in the *Venite* where the antiphon is recited.

With the exception of Lent, the Prayer Book supplies antiphons for all the seasons of the liturgical year. The Lenten antiphon, along with the chant tones for each season, can be found the Psalter.[^31] For simplicity, this Office commends chanting the antiphon throughout its appointed season rather than exclusively on its feast day. In this way, the *Venite* is always sung with an antiphon, in keeping with the ancient tradition.

The Psalter includes two endings for the *Venite*. The first is from the Prayer Book and is drawn from Psalm 96:9 and Psalm 96:13. The second is from Psalm 95 and may be used as an optional devotional practice for those who wish to chant the more ancient ending.

During the [Octave](/glossary/liturgical-terms#octave) of Easter, the [*Pascha nostrum*](/liturgy/music/chants/pascha-nostrum) replaces the *Venite;*[^32] and although the Prayer Book permits the omission of the *Venite* on Ash Wednesday, this Office retains it according to ancient precedent.[^33][^34] On days when the *Venite* is sung, Psalm 95 is to be omitted from the Psalms of the Day to avoid duplication.[^35]

## Psalms of the Day

Then come the Psalms of the Day.[^36] The appointed posture for chanting the psalms is seated, as it is properly a meditation.[^37]

Antiphonal psalmody traces its origins to the Jewish temple, and the early Christians continued on in the regular chanting of psalms.[^38] In the 6th century, the *Rule of Saint Benedict* outlined a seven-day psalter, so that by the end of each week all 150 Psalms would have been chanted—a task, Benedict notes, that his predecessors had accomplished within a single day.[^39] In order to make the Office more accessible to the laity, however, the Prayer Book adopted a monthly psalter,[^40] whose arrangement has remained unaltered since it was settled in the early 1540s.[^41]

## First Lesson

Then comes the First Lesson from the Old Testament. Ordinarily, the Reader stands and approaches the [lectern](/glossary/liturgical-terms#lectern). Before reading, he says, "Here beginneth the *nth* verse of the *nth* chapter of the Book of *N*." After reading, he says, "Here endeth the *Nth* Lesson."[^42]

The lessons for this Office follow the Prayer Book's one-year [lectionary](/glossary/liturgical-terms#lectionary) and therefore may not always correspond precisely to the feast or Sunday.[^43] The Epistle and Gospel appointed for the feast or Sunday are read at Holy Communion.

## Te Deum

At Morning Prayer, the First Lesson is followed by the singing of the [*Te Deum*](/liturgy/music/chants/te-deum).[^44] The *Te Deum*, also called the "Ambrosian Hymn," is an ancient hymn dating back to the 4th century, or perhaps even earlier.[^45] In the 6th century, it was prescribed for Matins on Lord's Days in the *Rule of Saint Benedict*.[^46] The appointed posture for the *Te Deum* and the Canticles is standing.[^47] According to the Use of Sarum, the *Te Deum* is sung on the following occasions:

- **All Feasts and Lord's Days**
- **Daily during:**
  - Christmastide
  - Octave of Epiphany
  - Eastertide, except on [Rogation Days](/liturgy/liturgical-year/seasons/eastertide/rogation-days)
  - Whitsuntide, except on [Ember Days](/glossary/liturgical-terms#ember-days)
- **Omitted during:**
  - Advent
  - The Holy Innocents, unless it falls on a Sunday
  - From Septuagesima to Easter (exclusive)
  - All Souls' Day[^48][^49]

## Old Testament Canticles

This Office appoints the *Benedicite* on days when the *Te Deum* is not sung.[^50] The *Benedicite* belongs to the ancient cycle of [Old Testament Canticles](/liturgy/daily-office/psalter#old-testament-canticles) sung throughout the week at [Lauds](/glossary/liturgical-terms#lauds).[^51] As an optional devotional practice, one may simply chant through the cycle on days when the *Te Deum* is not sung. The Old Testament Canticles are provided in the Psalter.[^52] Similarly, those comfortable singing in Latin may wish to follow the Benedictine practice of singing the *Benedicite* on Christmas Day with the antiphon [*Facta est cum Angelo*](/liturgy/music/chants/facta-est-cum-angelo) sung before and after.[^53]

## Second Lesson

Then comes the Second Lesson from the New Testament, read according to the same rubrics as the First Lesson.[^54]

## Gospel Canticles

[***Benedictus***](/liturgy/music/chants/benedictus)

*The Rule of Saint Benedict* prescribed a Gospel Canticle after the Lessons at Lauds,[^55] and in 820 AD, Amalarius, Frankish bishop and contemporary of Charlemagne, attested to the use of the *Benedictus* as this canticle.[^56] Accordingly, the Prayer Book places the *Benedictus* after the Second Lesson at Morning Prayer.[^57] This placement is fitting because, like [St. John the Baptist](/people/saints/biblical/john-the-baptist)—about whom this canticle is written—it links together the Old and New Testaments.

It is customary to make the Sign of the Cross at the beginning of each Gospel Canticle.[^58] Although the Prayer Book permits certain omissions and substitutions, this Office preserves all the Canticles in full at their appointed places. A canticle may be both read and sung in the same Office, provided it is neither read twice nor sung twice.

[***Magnificat***](/liturgy/music/chants/magnificat)

*The Rule of Saint Benedict* also prescribed a Gospel Canticle after the Lesson at [Vespers](/glossary/liturgical-terms#vespers),[^59] and Amalarius attested to the use of the *Magnificat* as this canticle.[^60] Accordingly, the Prayer Book places the *Magnificat* after the First Lesson at Evening Prayer.[^61] As with the *Benedictus*, this placement is fitting because, like [Our Lady](/people/saints/biblical/blessed-virgin-mary)—who first proclaimed the words of this canticle—it links together the Old and New Testaments.

Traditionally, the [O Antiphons](/liturgy/liturgical-year/seasons/advent/o-antiphons) were sung before and after the *Magnificat* at Vespers during the last eight nights of Advent.[^62] Accordingly, this Office uses the verses of the English paraphrase—[*O Come, O Come, Emmanuel*](/liturgy/music/hymns/o-come-o-come-emmanuel)—as antiphons before and after the *Magnificat* at Evening Prayer on these nights.[^63] Five of the verses are drawn from [*Hymns Ancient & Modern*](/glossary/liturgical-terms#hymns-ancient--modern) (1861).[^64] The verses for [*O Sapientia*](/liturgy/music/chants/o-sapientia) and [*O Rex gentium*](/liturgy/music/chants/o-rex-gentium)—which were paraphrased later—are taken from *The Hymnal of the Episcopal Church* (1940).[^65] The paraphrase of [*O Virgo virginum*](/liturgy/music/chants/o-virgo-virginum)—the eighth O Antiphon according to the medieval English use—is unique to this Office. The verses are sung to the traditional melody, the earliest surviving manuscripts of which date back to the 16th century.[^66]

[***Nunc dimittis***](/liturgy/music/chants/nunc-dimittis)

The *Nunc dimittis* has been used as an evening prayer since at least the 4th century, when it was included in the *Apostolic Constitutions*.[^67] Amalarius attested to its use in the Office after the Lesson at Compline.[^68] Accordingly, the Prayer Book places the *Nunc dimittis* after the Second Lesson at Evening Prayer.[^69] This placement is fitting because, like Simeon—who, when first reciting this canticle, beheld the face of the child King—we end the day beholding the Lord in his incarnate glory.

As an optional devotional practice, those comfortable singing in Latin may follow the Benedictine practice of singing the *Nunc dimittis* on Lord's Days with the antiphon [*Salva nos Domine*](/liturgy/music/chants/salva-nos-domine) sung before and after.[^70]

## Optional Third Lesson

The Psalter suggests an optional Third Lesson after the Gospel Canticles for the commemoration of saints.[^71] Lessons have been provided for this purpose and are drawn from the 1897 edition of [*Lives of the Saints*](/glossary/liturgical-terms#lives-of-the-saints).[^72] The Third Lesson is read in the same manner as the other Lessons, except the Reader introduces the passage by saying, "Here beginneth a reading on the life of *N*."

## Concluding Rites
---

## Apostles' Creed

The Concluding Rites of the Office begin with the [Apostles' Creed](/liturgy/creeds/apostles-creed).[^73] The Apostles' Creed is one of the most enduring and widely used Christian confessions of faith, tracing its roots back to as early as the 2nd century. It developed from the Old Roman Creed, reaching its present form by the 8th century.[^74] The recitation of the Apostles' Creed at Morning and Evening Prayer reflects its ancient use at Prime and Compline.[^75] During the Creed, all stand facing the altar, the Officiant standing [*junctis manibus*](/glossary/liturgical-terms#junctis-manibus). At the Name of Jesus, all bow the head. At the conclusion of the Creed, all make the Sign of the Cross.[^76]

## Salutation

After the Apostles' Creed, the choir returns to its lateral position. The priest or deacon then turns to the congregation and recites the [Salutation](/glossary/liturgical-terms#salutation), extending and then joining his hands.[^77][^78] If the Officiant is not a priest or deacon, he recites the following from his lateral position:

<Poetry>
|  \V O Lord, hear our prayer.
|  \R And let our cry come unto thee.
|  \V Let us pray.[^79]
</Poetry>

## Suffrages

Immediately following the Salutation, all kneel for the recitation of the [Suffrages](/glossary/liturgical-terms#suffrages).[^80] If the Officiant is a priest or deacon, he may continue to stand.[^81]

The Suffrages are intercessions meant to serve as an active response to [St. Paul's](/people/saints/apostles/paul) exhortation in his First Epistle to Timothy: "I urge that supplications, prayers, intercessions, and thanksgivings be made for all people, for kings and all who are in high positions, that we may lead a peaceful and quiet life, godly and dignified in every way. This is good, and it is pleasing in the sight of God our Savior, who desires all people to be saved and to come to the knowledge of the truth" (1 Tim. 2:1-4).

## Collects

After the Suffrages, the Officiant recites the Collects. The *Rule of Saint Benedict* prescribed that the Day Hours end with the "dismissals".[^82] The nature of these "dismissals" is unclear, but in 820 AD, Amalarius attested to the use of the Suffrages and the Collect at the end of the Office.[^83] The Suffrages and the Collect likewise appear at the end of the Office in the Use of Sarum.[^84]

According to the Psalter, the Collects are chanted either using the [festal tone](/liturgy/daily-office/chant-rubrics#the-collects) or, on [ferial](/glossary/liturgical-terms#ferial) days, *<Definition lang="latin" anchor="recto-tono" text="recto tono" />*.[^85] The Collects of the Day are chanted first, followed by the Collects belonging to the Ordinary: *For Peace* and *For Grace* at Morning Prayer and *For Peace* and *For Aid Against Perils* at Evening Prayer.[^86]

The Collects for this Office are drawn principally from the Prayer Book.[^87] When the Prayer Book does not provide one, the appointed collect is drawn from the 1880 edition of [*Breviary Offices*](/glossary/liturgical-terms#breviary-offices).[^88] Collects for the commemoration of saints are drawn from the 2024 edition of [*Lesser Feasts and Fasts*](/glossary/liturgical-terms#lesser-feasts-and-fasts).[^89]

## The Anthem

After the Collects at Evening Prayer, the Prayer Book includes the rubric, "In places where it may be convenient, here followeth the <Definition lang="greek" anchor="antiphona" text="Anthem" />."[^90] Many scholars believe this refers to the seasonal Marian Antiphons, which were sung at the end of Compline in the medieval Roman Office. These antiphons can be found in the Psalter.[^91]

## Concluding Prayers

The Prayer Book originally ended the Office with the Collects.[^92] In 1662, a series of concluding prayers was added, including *A Prayer for the King's Majesty*, *A Prayer for the Royal Family*, and *A Prayer for the Clergy and People*.[^93] The American Prayer Book replaced the prayers for the King and Royal Family with *A Prayer for the President and All in Civil Authority*. The 1928 Prayer Book permitted the Officiant to use these prayers at his discretion.[^94]

## The Grace

Finally, the Grace (2 Cor. 13:14) is recited by the Officiant to conclude the Office.[^95] The Grace serves as a prayer for the gifts of the Three Persons of the Trinity, and has been used as a closing doxology for the Office since 1662.[^96]

The Office thus begins with repentance, moves through the singing and hearing of God's Word, and ends with a petition for divine favor in the words of the Apostle. The Sign of the Cross is made at the Grace, marking the conclusion of the Office and serving as a visible prayer for God's continuing presence.[^97]

## After the Office
---

According to the *Rule of Saint Benedict*, whatever time remains after Morning Prayer is to be devoted to thoughtful study of the Psalms and Lessons.[^98] It is also appropriate to include here the study of hymns, the Church Fathers, and the lives of the saints.
