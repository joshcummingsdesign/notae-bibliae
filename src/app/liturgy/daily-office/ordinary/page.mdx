<Initial text="Daily Office: Ordinary" />

The <Definition lang="latin" anchor="ordinarius" text="Ordinary" /> sets forth the fixed order of the Daily Office along with the customary manner in which it is celebrated.

## Before the Office
---

## Ceremonial

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text="❡ Such Ornaments of the Church, and of the Ministers thereof at all times of their ministration, shall be retained, and be in use, as were in this Church of England by the authority of Parliament, in the second year of the reign of King Edward the Sixth (BCP 1662, lxvi)." />
  <Rubric text="❡ The Minister at the time of the Communion, and at all other times in his ministration, shall use neither alb, vestment, nor cope, but being archbishop or bishop, he shall have and wear a rochet, and being a priest or deacon, he shall have and wear a surplice only (BCP 1552, 96)." />
</div>

<Red large={true} text="†" /> This Office commends the [cassock](/liturgy/vesture/clerical-dress#cassock) and [surplice](/liturgy/vesture/vestments#surplice) as the customary vesture of the ministers. Two altar candles are lighted in preparation for the Office.[^1] It is customary to kneel in silent prayer before the Office begins.[^2]

## Introductory Rites
---

## Sentences

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text="❡ The Minister shall begin ... by reading one or more of the following Sentences of Scripture (BCP 1928, 3, 21)." />
</div>

<Red large={true} text="†" /> The ministers proceed from the [sacristy](/glossary/liturgical-terms#sacristy) to the [chancel](/glossary/liturgical-terms#chancel), at which time all stand. The ministers take their [appointed places](/glossary/liturgical-terms#decani) in church, and the Officiant turns to the congregation and reads one or more of the Sentences. Any of the Sentences may be read at the discretion of the Officiant.[^3]

<Grey text="※" /> The Sentences are meant to furnish "sundry places" in which the Scripture moveth us to confess our sins before Almighty God with "an humble, lowly, penitent, and obedient heart; to the end that we may obtain forgiveness of the same, by his infinite goodness and mercy."

## Exhortation

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text='❡ Then the Minister shall say, "Dearly beloved brethren..." (BCP 1928, 5, 23).' />
</div>

<Red large={true} text="†" /> Still facing the congregation, the Officiant reads the Exhortation. All kneel at the words "accompany me."

<Grey text="※" /> The Exhortation outlines the theology of the Office: that we acknowledge our sins before God, set forth his most worthy praise, hear his most holy Word, and "ask those things which are requisite and necessary, as well for the body as the soul."

## General Confession

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text="❡ To be said by the whole congregation, after the Minister,\a all kneeling (BCP 1928, 6, 23)." />
</div>

<Grey text="※" /> The General Confession is an act of repentance in which we "with confidence draw near to the throne of grace, that we may receive mercy and find grace to help in time of need" (Heb. 4:16). The 1530 [*Augsburg Confession*](/meditations/luther-on-repentance) describes repentance as consisting of two parts: contrition and faith.[^4] Reciting the General Confession at Morning and Evening Prayer reflects the ancient practice of twice-daily confession at [Prime](/glossary/liturgical-terms#prime) and [Compline](/glossary/liturgical-terms#compline).[^5]

<ol type="a" style={{ margin: "1.5rem 0 2rem", fontSize: "0.9375rem" }}>
  <li>Some commentators say, "with the Minister," which is the practice of this Office.[^6] Note that here, as in all other instances, the *<Definition lang="hebrew" anchor="amen" text="Amen" />* is recited by the entire congregation. Because it forms part of a text recited by all, it is not italicized. When the Prayer Book italicizes *Amen*, it signifies the congregation's response to a prayer said by the Officiant.[^7]</li>
</ol>

## Absolution

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text="❡ To be made by the priest alone, standing; the people still kneeling (BCP 1928, 7, 24)." />
  <Rubric text="❡ If no priest be present the person saying the Service shall read the Collect for the Twenty-First Sunday after Trinity, that person and the people still kneeling (BCP 1662, Measure 1968, 4, 19)." />
</div>

<Red large={true} text="†" /> The priest stands and faces the congregation to pronounce the Absolution. The [Sign of the Cross](/liturgy/prayers/sign-of-the-cross) is made at the words, "He pardoneth and absolveth."[^8]

## Lord's Prayer

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text="❡ The Minister shall kneel, and say the Lord's Prayer; the people still kneeling, and repeating it with him (BCP 1928, 7, 24)." />
</div>

<Grey text="※" /> The Sarum Breviary states, "Before the beginning of the Hours, the [Lord's Prayer](/liturgy/prayers/our-father) is always said ... Whence [Jerome](/people/saints/latin-fathers/jerome-of-stridon), 'At the beginning of any work whatsoever, preface it with the Lord's Prayer and the Sign of the Cross on the forehead, according to what is written: Before prayer, prepare thy soul for the Lord; and be not as a man that tempteth God' (Ecclus. 18:23)."[^9]

The Lord's Prayer naturally follows the Absolution, for it is through the assurance of forgiveness in Christ that we find the confidence to address God as our heavenly Father. As the burning coal purified Isaiah's lips, so the Absolution cleanses us and prepares us to respond, "Here I am!" (Isa. 6:6-8), to the glory of God's holy Name.

## Office Proper
---

## Opening Versicles

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text='❡ Then likewise he shall say, "O Lord, open thou our lips..." (BCP 1928, 7, 25).' />
  <Rubric text='❡ Here, all standing up, the Minister shall say, "Glory be..."\a (BCP 1928, 8, 25).' />
</div>

<Red large={true} text="†" /> It is customary to make the Sign of the Cross on the lips with the thumb of the right hand at the words "O Lord, open thou our lips."<sup>[a]</sup>

<div style={{ margin: "1.5rem 0" }}>
  <p style={{ marginBottom: "0.5rem" }}>Omitted</p>
  <ul style={{ marginTop: 0 }}>
    <li>Last three days of Holy Week and All Souls' Day<sup>[b]</sup></li>
  </ul>
</div>

<Grey text="※" /> The 6th-century [*Rule of Saint Benedict*](/meditations/the-rule-of-saint-benedict) (RSB) prescribed two [versicles](/glossary/liturgical-terms#versicle) for the opening of [Matins](/glossary/liturgical-terms#matins): the [*Domine labia mea*](/liturgy/music/chants/domine-labia-mea) and the [*Deus in adjutorium*](/liturgy/music/chants/deus-in-adjutorium). This was to be followed by the [*Gloria Patri*](/liturgy/music/chants/gloria-patri) and the [*Alleluia*](/liturgy/music/chants/alleluia). The *Domine labia mea* was to be omitted the other hours of the day, and the *Alleluia* was to be omitted during certain times of the year.[^10] The Prayer Book simplified these rubrics by appointing the *Domine labia mea* for both Morning and Evening Prayer—as it flows more naturally into the praise of the *Gloria Patri* and the Invitatory—and by replacing the *Alleluia* with its English equivalent, "Praise ye the Lord," to be used throughout the year. It also added the response, "The Lord's Name be praised," thus allowing the entire opening to be chanted <Definition lang="english" anchor="antiphonal" text="antiphonally" />.[^11]

<ol type="a" style={{ margin: "1.5rem 0", fontSize: "0.9375rem" }}>
  <li>The RSB states that standing at the *Gloria Patri* is out of honor and reverence to the Holy Trinity.[^12] Accordingly, this Office prescribes standing any time the *Gloria Patri* is recited.</li>
  <li>The SDP directs that the Sign of the Cross be made on the lips.[^13] The Roman Breviary states that this is done with the thumb of the right hand.[^14]</li>
  <li>The Opening Versicles are traditionally omitted during the last three days of Holy Week and on All Souls' Day in recognition of the solemn character of these observances.[^15]</li>
</ol>

## Invitatory

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text="❡ Then shall be said or sung the following Canticle; except on those days for which other Canticles are appointed;\a and except also, that Psalm 95 may be used in this place\b (BCP 1928, 8)." />
  <Rubric text="❡ But note, that on Ash Wednesday and Good Friday the Venite may be omitted\c (BCP 1928, 8)." />
  <Rubric text='❡ On the days hereafter named, immediately before the Venite may be sung or said, On the Sundays in Advent. Our King and Saviour...\d (BCP 1928, 8).' />
</div>

<Red large={true} text="†" /> At Morning Prayer, all stand for the [*Venite*](/liturgy/music/chants/venite). The [Precentor](/glossary/liturgical-terms#precentor) begins by singing the [antiphon](/glossary/liturgical-terms#antiphon), and the choir repeats it. Then the Precentor sings the verses, and the choir sings the antiphon at the appointed places.[^16] Each of the provided antiphons is used for the entire season during which it is appointed, rather than exclusively on its feast day, Sunday, or [octave](/glossary/liturgical-terms#octave). In this way, the *Venite* is always chanted with an antiphon.

<div style={{ margin: "1.5rem 0" }}>
  <p style={{ marginBottom: "0.5rem" }}>Omitted</p>
  <ul style={{ marginTop: 0 }}>
    <li>Octave of Easter, [*Pascha nostrum*](/liturgy/music/chants/pascha-nostrum) sung instead (BCP 1928, 162)</li>
    <li>Last three days of Holy Week and All Souls' Day<sup>[e]</sup></li>
  </ul>
</div>

<Grey text="※" /> As the name implies, the [Invitatory](/glossary/liturgical-terms#invitatory) serves as a calling or invitation to praise. The RSB prescribed the *Venite* (Psalm 95) as the Invitatory for Matins and directed that it be sung with an antiphon.[^17] The exact manner of singing the Invitatory antiphon has varied over the centuries. The 15th-century *Myroure of Our Ladye* states that it is sung five times in full during the *Venite*, corresponding to the five senses, which those who serve the Lord must diligently guard.[^18] Accordingly, the [*Saint Dunstan's Psalter*](/glossary/liturgical-terms#saint-dunstans-plainsong-psalter) (SDP) marks five places in the *Venite* where the antiphon is to be recited.[^19] The last two verses of the *Venite* in the Prayer Book are drawn from Psalm 96:9 and Psalm 96:13. They emphasize the beauty of Christ and the Glory of his coming.

<ol type="a" style={{ margin: "1.5rem 0 2rem", fontSize: "0.9375rem" }}>
  <li>The "Canticle" here refers to Psalm 95, and "other Canticles" refers to the *Pascha nostrum*.</li>
  <li>This refers to when Psalm 95 shows up in the Psalms of the Day. This Office omits Psalm 95 from the Psalms of the Day rather than omitting the Invitatory.[^20]</li>
  <li>This Office retains the *Venite* on Ash Wednesday according to ancient precedent.[^21]</li>
  <li>The Prayer Book supplies antiphons for all the seasons of the liturgical year except for Lent. The Lenten antiphon can be found in the SDP.[^22]</li>
  <li>The Invitatory is traditionally omitted during the last three days of Holy Week and on All Souls' Day in recognition of the solemn character of these observances.[^23]</li>
</ol>

## Psalms of the Day

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text="❡ Then shall follow a Portion of the Psalms, according to the Use of this Church. And at the end of every Psalm, and likewise at the end of the Venite, Benedictus, ... shall be sung or said the Gloria Patri (BCP 1928, 9, 25)." />
</div>

<Red large={true} text="†" /> All sit for the chanting of the Psalms of the Day.[^24] With the exception of the *Benedicite*, every psalm and canticle is concluded with the *Gloria Patri*. All stand for the *Gloria Patri*.

<Grey text="※" /> Antiphonal psalmody traces its origins to the Jewish temple, and the early Christians continued on in the regular chanting of psalms. The RSB outlined a seven-day psalter, so that by the end of each week all 150 Psalms would have been chanted—a task, Benedict notes, that his predecessors had accomplished within a single day.[^25] In order to make the Office more accessible to the laity, the Prayer Book adopted a monthly psalter.[^26]
