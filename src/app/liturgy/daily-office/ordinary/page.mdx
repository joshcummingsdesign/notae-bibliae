<Initial text="Daily Office: Ordinary" />

The <Definition lang="latin" anchor="ordinarius" text="Ordinary" /> sets forth the fixed order of the Daily Office along with the customary manner in which it is celebrated.

## Before the Office
---

## Ceremonial

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text="❡ Such Ornaments of the Church, and of the Ministers thereof at all times of their ministration, shall be retained, and be in use, as were in this Church of England by the authority of Parliament, in the second year of the reign of King Edward the Sixth (BCP 1662, lxvi)." />
  <Rubric text="❡ The Minister at the time of the Communion, and at all other times in his ministration, shall use neither alb, vestment, nor cope, but being archbishop or bishop, he shall have and wear a rochet, and being a priest or deacon, he shall have and wear a surplice only (BCP 1552, 96)." />
</div>

<Red large={true} text="†" /> This Office commends the [cassock](/liturgy/vesture/clerical-dress#cassock) and [surplice](/liturgy/vesture/vestments#surplice) as the customary vesture of the ministers. Two altar candles are lighted in preparation for the Office.[^1] It is customary to kneel in silent prayer before the Office begins.[^2]

## Introductory Rites
---

## Sentences

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text="❡ The Minister shall begin the [Office] by reading one or more of the following Sentences of Scripture (BCP 1928, 3)." />
</div>

<Red large={true} text="†" /> The ministers proceed from the [sacristy](/glossary/liturgical-terms#sacristy) to the [chancel](/glossary/liturgical-terms#chancel), at which time all stand. The ministers take their [appointed places](/glossary/liturgical-terms#decani) in church, and the Officiant turns to the congregation and reads one or more of the Sentences. Any of the Sentences may be read at the discretion of the Officiant.[^3]

<Grey text="※" /> The Sentences are meant to furnish "sundry places" in which the Scripture moveth us to confess our sins before Almighty God with "an humble, lowly, penitent, and obedient heart; to the end that we may obtain forgiveness of the same, by his infinite goodness and mercy."

## Exhortation

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text='❡ Then the Minister shall say, "Dearly beloved brethren..." (BCP 1928, 5).' />
</div>

<Red large={true} text="†" /> Still facing the congregation, the Officiant reads the Exhortation. All kneel at the words "accompany me."

<Grey text="※" /> The Exhortation outlines the theology of the Office: that we are to acknowledge our sins before God, set forth his most worthy praise, hear his most holy Word, and "ask those things which are requisite and necessary, as well for the body as the soul."

## General Confession

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text="❡ To be said by the whole congregation, after the Minister,\a all kneeling (BCP 1928, 6)." />
</div>

<Grey text="※" /> The General Confession is an act of repentance in which we "with confidence draw near to the throne of grace, that we may receive mercy and find grace to help in time of need" (Heb. 4:16). The 1530 [*Augsburg Confession*](/meditations/luther-on-repentance) describes repentance as consisting of two parts: contrition and faith.[^4] Reciting the General Confession at Morning and Evening Prayer reflects the ancient practice of twice-daily confession at [Prime](/glossary/liturgical-terms#prime) and [Compline](/glossary/liturgical-terms#compline).[^5]

<ol type="a" style={{ margin: "1.5rem 0 2rem", fontSize: "0.9375rem" }}>
  <li>Some commentators say, "with the Minister," which is the practice of this Office.[^6] Note that here, as in all other instances, the *<Definition lang="hebrew" anchor="amen" text="Amen" />* is recited by the entire congregation. Because it forms part of a text recited by all, it is not italicized. When the Prayer Book italicizes *Amen*, it signifies the congregation's response to a prayer said by the Officiant.[^7]</li>
</ol>

## Absolution

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text="❡ To be made by the priest alone, standing; the people still kneeling (BCP 1928, 7)." />
  <Rubric text="❡ If no priest be present the person saying the Service shall read the Collect for the Twenty-First Sunday after Trinity, that person and the people still kneeling (BCP 1662, 4; Measure 1968)." />
</div>

<Red large={true} text="†" /> The priest stands and faces the congregation to pronounce the Absolution. The [Sign of the Cross](/liturgy/prayers/sign-of-the-cross) is made at the words, "He pardoneth and absolveth."[^8]

## Lord's Prayer

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text="❡ Then the Minister shall kneel, and say the Lord's Prayer; the people still kneeling, and repeating it with him (BCP 1928, 7)." />
</div>

<Grey text="※" /> The [Sarum Breviary](/liturgy/rites/sarum-rite) states, "Before the beginning of the Hours, the [Lord's Prayer](/liturgy/prayers/our-father) is always said ... Whence [Jerome](/people/saints/latin-fathers/jerome-of-stridon), 'At the beginning of any work whatsoever, preface it with the Lord's Prayer and the Sign of the Cross on the forehead, according to what is written: Before prayer, prepare thy soul for the Lord; and be not as a man that tempteth God' (Ecclus. 18:23)."[^9]

The Lord's Prayer naturally follows the Absolution, for it is through the assurance of forgiveness in Christ that we find the confidence to address God as our heavenly Father. As the burning coal purified Isaiah's lips, so the Absolution cleanses us and prepares us to respond, "Here I am!" (Isa. 6:6-8), to the glory of God's holy Name.

## Office Proper
---

## Opening Versicles

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text='❡ Then likewise he shall say, "O Lord, open thou our lips..." (BCP 1928, 7).' />
  <Rubric text='❡ Here, all standing up, the Minister shall say, "Glory be..."\a (BCP 1928, 8).' />
</div>

<Red large={true} text="†" /> It is customary to make the Sign of the Cross on the lips with the thumb of the right hand at the words "O Lord, open thou our lips."<sup>[b]</sup>

<div style={{ margin: "1.5rem 0" }}>
  <p style={{ marginBottom: "0.5rem" }}>Omitted</p>
  <ul style={{ marginTop: 0 }}>
    <li>Last three days of Holy Week and All Souls' Day<sup>[c]</sup></li>
  </ul>
</div>

<Grey text="※" /> The 6th-century [*Rule of Saint Benedict*](/meditations/the-rule-of-saint-benedict) (RSB) prescribed two [versicles](/glossary/liturgical-terms#versicle) for the opening of [Matins](/glossary/liturgical-terms#matins): the [*Domine labia mea*](/liturgy/music/chants/domine-labia-mea) and the [*Deus in adjutorium*](/liturgy/music/chants/deus-in-adjutorium). This was to be followed by the [*Gloria Patri*](/liturgy/music/chants/gloria-patri) and the [*Alleluia*](/liturgy/music/chants/alleluia). The *Domine labia mea* was to be omitted the other hours of the day, and the *Alleluia* was to be omitted during certain times of the year.[^10] The Prayer Book simplified these rubrics by appointing the *Domine labia mea* for both Morning and Evening Prayer—as it flows more naturally into the praise of the *Gloria Patri* and the Invitatory—and by replacing the *Alleluia* with its English equivalent, "Praise ye the Lord," to be used throughout the year. It also added the response, "The Lord's Name be praised," thus allowing the entire opening to be chanted <Definition lang="english" anchor="antiphonal" text="antiphonally" />.[^11]

<ol type="a" style={{ margin: "1.5rem 0", fontSize: "0.9375rem" }}>
  <li>This Office follows the ancient English custom of standing whenever the *Gloria Patri* is recited.[^12]</li>
  <li>The SDP directs that the Sign of the Cross be made on the lips.[^13] The Roman Breviary states that this is done with the thumb of the right hand.[^14]</li>
  <li>The Opening Versicles are traditionally omitted during the last three days of Holy Week and on All Souls' Day in recognition of the solemn character of these observances.[^15]</li>
</ol>

## Invitatory

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text="❡ Then shall be said or sung the following Canticle; except on those days for which other Canticles are appointed;\a and except also, that Psalm 95 may be used in this place\b (BCP 1928, 8)." />
  <Rubric text="❡ But note, that on Ash Wednesday and Good Friday the Venite may be omitted\c (BCP 1928, 8)." />
  <Rubric text='❡ On the days hereafter named, immediately before the Venite may be sung or said, On the Sundays in Advent. Our King and Saviour...\d (BCP 1928, 8).' />
</div>

<Red large={true} text="†" /> At Morning Prayer, all stand for the [*Venite*](/liturgy/music/chants/venite). The [Precentor](/glossary/liturgical-terms#precentor) begins by singing the [antiphon](/glossary/liturgical-terms#antiphon), and the choir repeats it. Then the Precentor sings the verses, and the choir sings the antiphon at the appointed places.[^16] Each of the provided antiphons is used for the entire season during which it is appointed, rather than exclusively on its feast day, Sunday, or [octave](/glossary/liturgical-terms#octave). In this way, the *Venite* is always chanted with an antiphon.

<div style={{ margin: "1.5rem 0" }}>
  <p style={{ marginBottom: "0.5rem" }}>Omitted</p>
  <ul style={{ marginTop: 0 }}>
    <li>Octave of Easter, [*Pascha nostrum*](/liturgy/music/chants/pascha-nostrum) sung instead (BCP 1928, 162)</li>
    <li>Last three days of Holy Week and All Souls' Day<sup>[e]</sup></li>
  </ul>
</div>

<Grey text="※" /> As the name implies, the [Invitatory](/glossary/liturgical-terms#invitatory) serves as a calling or invitation to praise. The RSB prescribed the *Venite* (Psalm 95) as the Invitatory for Matins and directed that it be sung with an antiphon.[^17] The exact manner of singing the Invitatory antiphon has varied over the centuries. The 15th-century *Myroure of Our Ladye* states that it is sung five times in full during the *Venite*, corresponding to the five senses, which those who serve the Lord must diligently guard.[^18] Accordingly, the [*Saint Dunstan's Psalter*](/glossary/liturgical-terms#saint-dunstans-plainsong-psalter) (SDP) marks five places in the *Venite* where the antiphon is to be recited.[^19] The last two verses of the *Venite* in the Prayer Book are drawn from Psalm 96:9 and Psalm 96:13. They emphasize the beauty of Christ and the Glory of his coming.

<ol type="a" style={{ margin: "1.5rem 0 2rem", fontSize: "0.9375rem" }}>
  <li>The "Canticle" here refers to Psalm 95, and "other Canticles" refers to the *Pascha nostrum*.</li>
  <li>This refers to when Psalm 95 shows up in the Psalms of the Day. This Office omits Psalm 95 from the Psalms of the Day rather than omitting the Invitatory.[^20]</li>
  <li>This Office retains the *Venite* on Ash Wednesday according to ancient precedent.[^21]</li>
  <li>The Prayer Book supplies antiphons for all the seasons of the liturgical year except for Lent. The Lenten antiphon can be found in the SDP.[^22]</li>
  <li>The Invitatory is traditionally omitted during the last three days of Holy Week and on All Souls' Day in recognition of the solemn character of these observances.[^23]</li>
</ol>

## Psalms of the Day

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text="❡ Then shall follow a Portion of the Psalms, according to the Use of this Church. And at the end of every Psalm, and likewise at the end of the Venite, Benedictus, ... shall be sung or said the Gloria Patri (BCP 1928, 9)." />
</div>

<Red large={true} text="†" /> All sit for the chanting of the Psalms of the Day.[^24] With the exception of the *Benedicite*, every psalm and canticle is concluded with the *Gloria Patri*. All stand for the *Gloria Patri*.

<Grey text="※" /> Antiphonal psalmody traces its origins to the Jewish temple, and the early Christians continued on in the regular chanting of psalms. The RSB outlined a seven-day psalter, so that by the end of each week all 150 Psalms would have been chanted—a task, [St. Benedict](/people/saints/medieval/benedict-of-nursia) notes, that his predecessors had accomplished within a single day.[^25] In order to make the Office more accessible to the laity, the Prayer Book adopted a monthly psalter.[^26]

## First Lesson

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text='❡ Then shall be read the First Lesson, according to the Table or Calendar.\a ... Before every Lesson, the Minister shall say, "Here beginneth such a chapter (or verse of such a chapter) of such a book;" and after every Lesson, "Here endeth the First (or Second) Lesson (BCP 1928, 9).' />
</div>

<Red large={true} text="†" /> All sit for the reading of the First Lesson from the Old Testament.<sup>[b]</sup> The Reader stands and approaches the [lectern](/glossary/liturgical-terms#lectern). Before reading, he says, "Here beginneth the *nth* chapter of the Book of *N*, beginning at the *nth* verse." After reading, he says, "Here endeth the *Nth* Lesson."

<ol type="a" style={{ margin: "1.5rem 0 2rem", fontSize: "0.9375rem" }}>
  <li>The lessons for the Office follow the Prayer Book's one-year [lectionary](/glossary/liturgical-terms#lectionary) and therefore may not always correspond precisely to the feast or Sunday. The Epistle and Gospel appointed for the feast or Sunday are read at Holy Communion. For simplicity, canticles may be both read and sung in the same Office, provided they are neither read twice nor sung twice.</li>
  <li>The proper posture for the hearing of the Lessons is seated.[^27]</li>
</ol>

## Te Deum

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text="❡ Here shall be said or sung the following Hymn (BCP 1928, 11)." />
</div>

<Red large={true} text="†" /> At Morning Prayer, all stand for the [*Te Deum*](/liturgy/music/chants/te-deum).<sup>[a]</sup>

<div style={{ margin: "1.5rem 0" }}>
  <p style={{ marginBottom: "0.5rem" }}>Daily</p>
  <ul style={{ marginTop: 0 }}>
    <li>All Feasts and Lord's Days</li>
    <li>Octave of Epiphany</li>
    <li>Eastertide, except on Rogation Days</li>
    <li>Whitsuntide, except on Ember Days</li>
  </ul>
  <p style={{ marginBottom: "0.5rem" }}>Omitted</p>
  <ul style={{ marginTop: 0 }}>
    <li>Advent</li>
    <li>The Holy Innocents, unless it falls on a Sunday</li>
    <li>From Septuagesima to Easter (exclusive)</li>
    <li>All Souls' Day[^28][^29]</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<Grey text="※" /> The *Te Deum* is an ancient hymn. Some manuscripts attribute it to [St. Ambrose](/people/saints/latin-fathers/ambrose-of-milan) and [St. Augustine](/people/saints/latin-fathers/augustine-of-hippo), giving rise to the belief that it was composed for Augustine's baptism in 386 AD. More likely, however, their names became associated with the hymn because they helped introduce it into the Western Church. Scholars generally agree that, in its present form, the hymn was composed around the 4th century.[^30] The RSB prescribed the *Te Deum* for Matins on Lord's Days.[^31]

<ol type="a" style={{ margin: "1.5rem 0 2rem", fontSize: "0.9375rem" }}>
  <li>The proper posture for the *Te Deum* and all the canticles is standing.[^32]</li>
</ol>

## Benedicite

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text="❡ Or this Canticle (BCP 1928, 11)." />
</div>

<Red large={true} text="†" /> At Morning Prayer, when the *Te Deum* is not sung, all stand for the [*Benedicite*](/liturgy/music/chants/benedicite). The *Gloria Patri* is not recited at the end of the *Benedicite*, as it ends with its own [doxology](/glossary/liturgical-terms#doxology).<sup>[a]</sup>

<Grey text="※" /> The words of the *Benedicite* come from the *Prayer of Azariah and Song of the Three Holy Children*, which is found in the [Septuagint](/bible/translations/septuagint) version of Daniel 3 and was included in the [Vulgate](/bible/translations/vulgate). The *Benedicite* belongs to the ancient weekly cycle of [Old Testament Canticles](/liturgy/daily-office/psalter#old-testament-canticles) recited at [Lauds](/glossary/liturgical-terms#lauds), where it was appointed for Lord's Days.[^33]

<ol type="a" style={{ margin: "1.5rem 0 2rem", fontSize: "0.9375rem" }}>
  <li>The last two verses of the *Benedicite*, which form the canticle's doxology, were replaced with the *Gloria Patri* in the 1549 Prayer Book (BCP 1549, 5). The first half, "Let us bless the Father," was restored in 1928, but the second half, along with the verse, "O Ananias, Azarias, and Misael," were omitted. The Roman Rite expressly states that the *Gloria Patri* is not said.[^34]</li>
</ol>

## Magnificat

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text="❡ After which shall be sung or said the Hymn called Magnificat (BCP 1928, 26)." />
</div>

<Red large={true} text="†" /> At Evening Prayer, all stand for the [*Magnificat*](/liturgy/music/chants/magnificat). The verses of [*O Come, O Come, Emmanuel*](/liturgy/music/hymns/o-come-o-come-emmanuel) are distributed and sung as antiphons during the last eight nights of Advent.<sup>[a]</sup>

<Grey text="※" /> The RSB prescribed a Gospel Canticle after the Lesson at [Vespers](/glossary/liturgical-terms#vespers).[^35] In 820 AD, Amalarius, Frankish bishop and contemporary of Charlemagne, attested to the use of the *Magnificat* as this canticle.[^36] Accordingly, the Prayer Book places the *Magnificat* after the First Lesson at Evening Prayer. This placement is fitting because, like [Our Lady](/people/saints/biblical/blessed-virgin-mary)—who first proclaimed the words of this canticle—it links together the Old and New Testaments. The [O Antiphons](/liturgy/liturgical-year/seasons/advent/o-antiphons) have long accompanied the Magnificat during the last eight nights of Advent.[^37]

<ol type="a" style={{ margin: "1.5rem 0 2rem", fontSize: "0.9375rem" }}>
  <li>Five of the verses of *O Come, O Come, Emmanuel* are drawn from [*Hymns Ancient & Modern*](/glossary/liturgical-terms#hymns-ancient--modern) (1861).[^38] The verses for [*O Sapientia*](/liturgy/music/chants/o-sapientia) and [*O Rex gentium*](/liturgy/music/chants/o-rex-gentium)—which were paraphrased later—are taken from *The Hymnal of the Episcopal Church* (1940).[^39] The paraphrase of [*O Virgo virginum*](/liturgy/music/chants/o-virgo-virginum)—the eighth O Antiphon according to the medieval English use—is unique to this Office. The verses are sung to the traditional melody, the earliest surviving manuscripts of which date back to the 15th century.[^40]</li>
</ol>

## Benedictus

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text="❡ And after that shall be sung or said the Hymn following (BCP 1928, 14)." />
</div>

<Red large={true} text="†" /> At Morning Prayer, all stand for the [*Benedictus*](/liturgy/music/chants/benedictus).

<Grey text="※" /> The RSB prescribed a Gospel Canticle after the Lessons at Lauds.[^41] In 820 AD, Amalarius attested to the use of the *Benedictus* as this canticle.[^42] Accordingly, the Prayer Book places the *Benedictus* after the Second Lesson at Morning Prayer. This placement is fitting because, like [St. John the Baptist](/people/saints/biblical/john-the-baptist)—about whom this canticle is written—it links together the Old and New Testaments.

## Nunc dimittis

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text="❡ And after that shall be sung or said the Hymn called Nunc dimittis (BCP 1928, 28)." />
</div>

<Red large={true} text="†" /> At Evening Prayer, all stand for the [*Nunc dimittis*](/liturgy/music/chants/nunc-dimittis).

<Grey text="※" /> The *Nunc dimittis* has been used as an evening prayer since at least the 4th century, when it was included in the *Apostolic Constitutions*.[^43] In 820 AD, Amalarius attested to its use in the Office after the Lesson at Compline.[^44] Accordingly, the Prayer Book places the *Nunc dimittis* after the Second Lesson at Evening Prayer. This placement is fitting because, like Simeon—who, when first reciting this canticle, beheld the face of the child King—we end the day beholding the Lord in his incarnate glory.

## Optional Third Lesson

<Red large={true} text="†" /> The SDP suggests an optional Third Lesson after the Gospel Canticles for the commemoration of saints.[^45] Lessons have been provided for this purpose and are drawn from the 1897 edition of [*Lives of the Saints*](/glossary/liturgical-terms#lives-of-the-saints).[^46] The Third Lesson is read in the same manner as the other Lessons, except that the Reader introduces the passage by saying, "Here beginneth a reading on the life of *N*."

## Concluding Rites
---

## Apostles' Creed

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text="❡ Then shall be said the Apostles' Creed by the Minister and the people, standing (BCP 1928, 15)." />
</div>

<Red large={true} text="†" /> All stand and face the altar for the [Apostles' Creed](/liturgy/creeds/apostles-creed).[^47] It is customary to bow the head at the Name of Jesus, and to make the Sign of the Cross at the end of the Creed.[^48]

<Grey text="※" /> The Apostles' Creed is one of the most enduring and widely used Christian confessions of faith, tracing its roots back to as early as the 2nd century. It developed from the Old Roman Creed, reaching its present form by the 8th century.[^49] The recitation of the Apostles' Creed at Morning and Evening Prayer reflects its ancient use at Prime and Compline.[^50]

## Salutation

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text='❡ The Minister first pronouncing, "The Lord be with you..." (BCP 1928, 16).' />
</div>

<Red large={true} text="†" /> The choir returns to its lateral position, and the priest or deacon turns to the congregation and recites the [Salutation](/glossary/liturgical-terms#salutation). If the Officiant is not a priest or deacon, he remains in his lateral position and instead recites, ["O Lord, hear our prayer."](/glossary/liturgical-terms#o-lord-hear-my-prayer)[^51]

<Grey text="※" /> The Salutation has been pronounced by ordained men since ancient times. It is commonly used to introduce formal prayers. Its origin is scriptural (Ruth 2:4), and many believe it to be apostolic.[^52] The versicle is a blessing directed to the congregation, and the response is a prayer that the Lord, by the Holy Spirit, would bless the priest or deacon in his vocation. The alternative, "O Lord, hear our prayer," is also ancient and is taken from Psalm 102:1. It is used in many places throughout the Prayer Book (BCP 1928, 62). The injunction to use it in place of the Salutation comes from the Roman Breviary.[^53]

## Suffrages

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text="❡ And after that, these Prayers following, the people devoutly kneeling (BCP 1928, 16)." />
</div>

<Red large={true} text="†" /> All kneel for the [Suffrages](/glossary/liturgical-terms#suffrages) and remain kneeling until the end of the Office. If the Officiant is a priest or deacon, he may continue to stand.[^54]

<Grey text="※" /> The Suffrages are intercessions meant to serve as an active response to [St. Paul's](/people/saints/apostles/paul) exhortation from his First Epistle to Timothy: "I urge that supplications, prayers, intercessions, and thanksgivings be made for all people, for kings and all who are in high positions, that we may lead a peaceful and quiet life, godly and dignified in every way. This is good, and it is pleasing in the sight of God our Savior, who desires all people to be saved and to come to the knowledge of the truth" (1 Tim. 2:1-4).

## Collects

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text="Then shall be said the Collect for the Day, and after that the Collects (BCP 1928, 31)." />
</div>

<Red large={true} text="†" /> The Officiant chants the [Collects](/glossary/liturgical-terms#collect). The Collects are chanted either (1) using the [festal tone](/liturgy/daily-office/chant-rubrics#the-collects), or (2) *<Definition lang="latin" anchor="recto-tono" text="recto tono" />* on [ferial](/glossary/liturgical-terms#ferial) days.[^55] The Collects of the Day are chanted first,<sup>[a]</sup> followed by the Collects belonging to the Ordinary.<sup>[b]</sup>

<Grey text="※" /> The RSB prescribed that the Day Hours end with the "dismissals."<sup>[c]</sup> The nature of these "dismissals" is unclear, but in 820 AD, Amalarius attested to the use of the Suffrages and Collect at the end of the Office.[^56] The Suffrages and Collect likewise appear at the end of the Office in the Sarum Use.[^57]

<ol type="a" style={{ margin: "1.5rem 0 2rem", fontSize: "0.9375rem" }}>
  <li>The Collects of the Day are drawn principally from the Prayer Book (BCP 1928, 90). When the Prayer Book does not provide one, the appointed Collect is drawn from the 1880 edition of [*Breviary Offices*](/glossary/liturgical-terms#breviary-offices).[^58] Collects for the commemoration of saints are drawn from the 2024 edition of [*Lesser Feasts and Fasts*](/glossary/liturgical-terms#lesser-feasts-and-fasts).[^59]</li>
  <li>The Collects belonging to the Ordinary are *For Pace* and *For Grace* at Morning Prayer, and *For Peace* and *For Aid Against Perils* at Evening Prayer.</li>
  <li>Some translators use the word "Collect", but the Latin is *<Definition lang="latin" anchor="missae" text="missae" />*, not *<Definition lang="latin" anchor="oratio" text="oratio" />*.[^60]</li>
</ol>

## Anthem

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text="❡ In places where it may be convenient, here followeth the Anthem (BCP 1928, 31)." />
</div>

<Red large={true} text="†" /> At Evening Prayer, an <Definition lang="greek" anchor="antiphona" text="Anthem" /> may be sung at the discretion of the Officiant.

<Grey text="※" /> The 1549 Prayer Book concluded the Office with the Collects (BCP 1549, 7). The 1662 Prayer Book introduced a rubric to allow for the singing of an Anthem, which many scholars believe corresponds to the antiphon used at the end of the pre-Reformation Office. The 1789 American Prayer Book did not include this rubric, but it was reintroduced for Evening Prayer in 1892.[^61]

## Concluding Prayers

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text="❡ The Minister may here end the [Office] with such Prayer, or Prayers, taken out of this Book, as he shall think fit (BCP 1928, 31)." />
</div>

<Red large={true} text="†" /> One or more concluding prayers may be used at the discretion of the Officiant.

<Grey text="※" /> Along with the Anthem rubric, the 1662 Prayer book introduced a series of concluding prayers at the end of the Office, such as *A Prayer for the King's Majesty*, *A Prayer for the Royal Family*, and *A Prayer for the Clergy and People* (BCP 1662, 14). The 1789 American Prayer Book replaced the prayers for the King and Royal Family with *A Prayer for The President of the United States, and all in Civil Authority*.[^62] The 1928 Prayer Book left the use of these prayers to the discretion of the Officiant.

## The Grace

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text="❡ The Minister may here end the [Office] with such general intercessions taken out of this Book, as he shall think fit, or with the Grace (BCP 1928, 17)." />
</div>

<Red large={true} text="†" /> The Officiant recites the Grace (2 Cor. 13:14) to conclude the Office. It is customary to make the Sign of the Cross at the beginning of the Grace.[^63]

<Grey text="※" /> The RSB prescribed that Matins and Compline conclude with a benediction.[^64] The 1662 Prayer Book appointed the Grace—the ancient benediction of St. Paul from his Second Epistle to the Corinthians—for the conclusion of the Office (BCP 1662, 15). As the name implies, the Grace serves as a prayer for "the grace of our Lord Jesus Christ, and the love of God, and the fellowship of the Holy Ghost" (2 Cor. 13:14).

The Office thus begins with repentance, moves through the singing and hearing of God's Word, and ends with a petition for divine favor in the words of the Apostle. The Sign of the Cross is made at the Grace, marking the conclusion of the Office and serving as a visible prayer for God's continuing presence.

## Time for Study

<Red large={true} text="†" /> According to the RSB, whatever time remains after Morning Prayer is to be devoted to thoughtful study of the Psalms and Lessons.[^65] It is also appropriate to include here the study of hymns, the Church Fathers, and the lives of the saints.
