<Initial text="Daily Office: Ordinary" />

The <Definition lang="latin" anchor="ordinarius" text="Ordinary" /> refers to the parts of the Daily Office that are generally unchanged regardless of the day or liturgical season.

## Before the Office
---

## Candles

Two white altar candles of pure wax are lit in preparation for the Office. Their light symbolizes Christ, the Light of the World (John 8:12); their number signifies his two natures, human and divine; and their color and purity reflect his holiness and purity.[^1]

## Silent Prayer

Before the Office, it is customary to kneel in silent prayer. The 15th-century [*Myroure of Our Ladye*](/meditations/silent-prayer-before-the-daily-office), written for the Bridgettine Sisters of Syon Abbey in West London, beautifully describes in detail the purpose of kneeling in silent prayer as a way of preparing one's heart and mind for the Office.[^2]

## Introductory Rites
---

## Opening Sentence

The Introductory Rites of the Office begin with the Opening Sentence.[^3] Ordinarily, the ministers proceed from the [sacristy](/glossary/liturgical-terms#sacristy) to the [chancel](/glossary/liturgical-terms#chancel), at which point all stand. The ministers bow or [genuflect](/glossary/liturgical-terms#genuflection) toward the altar and take their [appointed places](/glossary/liturgical-terms#decani) in the church. Then the Opening Sentence proper to the day is recited.[^4] The Opening Sentence is regarded as an [antiphon](/glossary/liturgical-terms#antiphon) which frames the entire Office; thus it is chanted *<Definition lang="latin" anchor="recto-tono" text="recto tono" />* by the Officiant from his place in the choir, facing laterally.[^5][^6] The Opening Sentences used in this Office come from the [*Saint Dunstan's Psalter*](/glossary/liturgical-terms#saint-dunstans-plainsong-psalter) (SDP).[^7] According to ancient precedent, all make the [Sign of the Cross](/liturgy/prayers/sign-of-the-cross) at the beginning of the Office.[^8][^9]

## General Confession

Immediately after the Opening Sentence, all kneel for the Exhortation, which is recited by the Officiant.[^10] If the Officiant is a priest or deacon, he may stand facing the congregation.[^11][^12] The Exhortation may be either chanted *recto tono* or simply read.[^13] In practice, however, the shorter Exhortation lends itself more readily to chanting than the original longer form.

After the Officiant gives the Exhortation, all recite the General Confession.[^14] The General Confession is an act of repentance in which we "with confidence draw near to the throne of grace, that we may receive mercy and find grace to help in time of need" (Heb. 4:16). The 1530 [*Augsburg Confession*](/meditations/luther-on-repentance) beautifully describes in detail the meaning and purpose of repentance.[^15] Reciting the General Confession at Morning and Evening Prayer reflects the ancient practice of twice-daily confession at [Prime](/glossary/liturgical-terms#prime) and [Compline](/glossary/liturgical-terms#compline).[^16]

The Prayer Book directs that the General Confession be said "after the Minister," meaning that the Officiant recites each clause and the congregation repeats it after him. Accordingly, each clause begins with a capital letter.[^17] Note that here, as in all other instances, the *Amen* is recited by the entire congregation. Because it forms part of a text recited by all, it is not italicized. When the Prayer Book italicizes *Amen*, it signifies the congregation's response to a prayer said by the Officiant.[^18]

After the General Confession, the Absolution is given by the priest alone, [*junctis manibus*](/glossary/liturgical-terms#junctis-manibus).[^19][^20] According to the Psalter, the priest makes the Sign of the Cross toward the congregation at the words, "He pardoneth and absolveth."[^21] This is traditionally done with a right cross made with two fingers stretched out and the thumb hidden within them.[^22] It also states that if a priest is not present, the [collect](/glossary/liturgical-terms#collect) for the Twenty-First Sunday After Trinity is recited instead.[^23]

## Our Father

If not already kneeling, the Officiant kneels with the congregation for the [Our Father](/liturgy/prayers/our-father), which serves as a liturgical response to the Absolution.[^24] As the burning coal purified Isaiah's lips, so the Absolution cleanses us and prepares us to respond, "Here I am!" (Isa. 6:6-8), to the glory of God's holy Name in worship. Having thus been forgiven, we are able to pray with confidence, "Our Father."

This order harmonizes with the [Use of Sarum](/liturgy/rites/sarum-rite), which states, "Before the beginning of the [Hours](/glossary/liturgical-terms#liturgy-of-the-hours), the Lord's Prayer is always said ... Whence [Jerome](/people/saints/latin-fathers/jerome-of-stridon), 'At the beginning of any work whatsoever, preface it with the Lord's Prayer and the Sign of the Cross on the forehead, according to what is written: Before prayer, prepare thy soul for the Lord; and be not as a man that tempteth God' (Ecclus. 18:23)."[^25]

The [*Myroure of Our Ladye*](/meditations/the-lords-prayer-an-exposition-of-the-our-father) beautifully describes in more detail the rich meaning of the Our Father.[^26] According to ancient precedent, the Sign of the Cross is made at the end of the Our Father.[^27]

## Office Proper
---

## Opening Versicles

The Office Proper begins with the reciting of the Opening Versicles.[^28] In the 6th century, the [*Rule of Saint Benedict*](/liturgy/rites/benedictine-rite/liturgy-of-the-hours) prescribed two [versicles](/glossary/liturgical-terms#versicle) for the opening of [Matins](/glossary/liturgical-terms#matins): the [*Domine labia mea*](/liturgy/music/chants/domine-labia-mea) and the [*Deus in adjutorium*](/liturgy/music/chants/deus-in-adjutorium). This was to be followed by the [*Gloria Patri*](/liturgy/music/chants/gloria-patri) and the [*Alleluia*](/liturgy/music/chants/alleluia). The *Domine labia mea* was to be omitted the other hours of the day, and the *Alleluia* was to be omitted during certain times of the year.[^29]

The Prayer Book simplified these rubrics by prescribing the *Domine labia mea* for both Morning and Evening Prayer, since it flows more naturally into the praise of the [Invitatory](/glossary/liturgical-terms/#invitatory), and by replacing the *Alleluia* with its English equivalent, "Praise ye the Lord," to be used throughout the year. It also added the response, "The Lord's Name be praised," thus allowing the entire opening to be chanted <Definition lang="english" anchor="antiphonal" text="antiphonally" />.[^30]

According to the Prayer Book, the *Domine labia mea* is recited still kneeling, and all stand for the *Gloria Patri*. According to ancient precedent, the Sign of the Cross is made on the lips with the right thumb when saying, "O Lord, open thou our lips," and unless the proper posture is seated, it is customary to bow toward the altar whenever reciting the *Groria Patri*.[^31][^32][^33]

According to the Use of Sarum, the Opening Versicles and the Invitatory are omitted on the last three days of Holy Week and on All Souls' Day, reflecting the solemn character of these observances.[^34]

## Invitatory

At Morning Prayer, the Invitatory (Psalm 95, the [*Venite*](/liturgy/music/chants/venite)) follows the Opening Versicles.[^35] The use of the *Venite* in Christian worship dates back to at least the 4th century, when it was attested to by [St. Augustine](/people/saints/latin-fathers/augustine-of-hippo),[^36] and its use at Matins was prescribed in the *Rule of Saint Benedict*.[^37]

Chant tones and antiphons for the *Venite*, appointed for the seasons throughout the year, can be found in the Psalter. The Psalter includes two endings for the *Venite*. The first ending, taken from the Prayer Book, is drawn from Psalm 96. The second ending comes directly from Psalm 95 and is therefore preferred.[^38]

According to the Psalter, on days when the *Venite* is sung, Psalm 95 should be omitted from the Psalms of the Day to avoid duplication.[^39] During the [Octave of Easter](/glossary/liturgical-terms#octave), the [*Pascha nostrum*](/liturgy/music/chants/pascha-nostrum) replaces the *Venite*.[^40] Although the Prayer Book allows for the omission of the *Venite* on Ash Wednesday,[^41] this Office retains it, following ancient precedent.[^42]

## Psalms of the Day

Then come the Psalms of the Day.[^43] The proper posture for chanting the psalms is seated, as it is properly a meditation.[^44]

Antiphonal psalmody traces its origins to the Jewish temple, and the early Christians continued on in the regular chanting of psalms.[^45] In the 6th century, the *Rule of Saint Benedict* outlined a seven-day psalter, so that by the end of each week all 150 Psalms would have been chanted—a task, Benedict notes, that his predecessors had accomplished within a single day.[^46] In order to make the Office more accessible to the laity, however, the Prayer Book adopted a monthly psalter, whose arrangement has remained unaltered since it was settled in the early 1540s.[^47][^48]

With the exception of the [*Benedicite*](/liturgy/music/chants/benedicite), which ends with its own Trinitarian [doxology](/glossary/liturgical-terms#doxology), this Office concludes every psalm and [canticle](/glossary/liturgical-terms#canticle) with the *Gloria Patri*, even when the Prayer Book permits an alternative.[^49][^50][^51]

## First Lesson

Then comes the First Lesson from the Old Testament. Ordinarily, the [lector](/glossary/liturgical-terms#lector) stands, approaches the [lectern](/glossary/liturgical-terms#lectern), bows or genuflects toward the altar, and faces the seated congregation to read.[^52] Before reading, the lector says, "Here beginneth the *nth* verse of the *nth* chapter of the Book of *N*." After reading, the lector says, "Here endeth the *Nth* Lesson."[^53]

The lessons for this Office follow the Prayer Book's one-year [lectionary](/glossary/liturgical-terms#lectionary) and therefore may not always correspond precisely to the feast or Sunday.[^54] The Epistle and Gospel appointed for the feast or Sunday are read at Holy Communion.

## Te Deum

At Morning Prayer, the First Lesson is followed by the singing of the [*Te Deum*](/liturgy/music/chants/te-deum).[^55] The *Te Deum*, also called the "Ambrosian Hymn," is an ancient hymn dating back to the 4th century, or perhaps even earlier.[^56] In the 6th century, it was prescribed for Matins on Lord's Days in the *Rule of Saint Benedict*.[^57] The proper posture for the *Te Deum* and the Canticles is standing. It is customary to bow toward the altar at three places: "Holy, Holy, Holy", "When thou tookest upon thee to ... be born of a Virgin", and "We worship thy Name ever world without end." It is also customary to genuflect at "We therefore pray thee ... whom thou hast redeemed with thy precious blood."[^58] According to the Use of Sarum, the *Te Deum* is sung on the following occasions:

- **All Feasts and Lord's Days**
- **Daily during:**
  - Christmastide
  - Octave of Epiphany
  - Eastertide, except on [Rogation Days](/liturgy/liturgical-year/seasons/eastertide/rogation-days)
  - Whitsuntide, except on [Ember Days](/glossary/liturgical-terms#ember-days)
- **Omitted during:**
  - Advent
  - The Holy Innocents, unless it falls on a Sunday
  - From Septuagesima to Easter (exclusive)
  - All Souls' Day[^59][^60]

## Old Testament Canticles

On days when the *Te Deum* is not sung, this Office replaces it with an [Old Testament Canticle](/liturgy/daily-office/psalter#old-testament-canticles). This reflects the ancient practice whereby the Old Testament Canticles were distributed and sung daily at [Lauds](/glossary/liturgical-terms#lauds),[^61] which in turn explains why the Prayer Book gives the *Benedicite* as an alternative to the *Te Deum*.[^62]

This Office preserves all the Canticles in full at their appointed places, and therefore allows for both the reading and singing of a canticle in the same Office.

As an optional devotional practice, those comfortable singing in Latin may follow the Benedictine practice of singing the *Benedicite* on Christmas Day with the antiphon [*Facta est cum Angelo*](/liturgy/music/chants/facta-est-cum-angelo) sung before and after.[^63]

## Second Lesson

Then comes the Second Lesson from the New Testament, read according to the same rubrics as the First Lesson.[^64]

## Gospel Canticles

[***Benedictus***](/liturgy/music/chants/benedictus)

*The Rule of Saint Benedict* prescribed a Gospel Canticle after the Lessons at Lauds,[^65] and in 820 AD, Amalarius, Frankish bishop and contemporary of Charlemagne, attested to the use of the *Benedictus* as this canticle.[^66] Accordingly, the Prayer Book places the *Benedictus* after the Second Lesson at Morning Prayer.[^67] This placement is fitting because, like [St. John the Baptist](/people/saints/biblical/john-the-baptist)—about whom this canticle is written—it links together the Old and New Testaments.

Although the Prayer Book permits certain omissions or substitutions, this Office preserves all the Gospel Canticles in full at their appointed places, and therefore allows for both the reading and singing of a canticle in the same Office. It is customary to make the Sign of the Cross at the beginning of each Gospel Canticle.[^68]

[***Magnificat***](/liturgy/music/chants/magnificat)

*The Rule of Saint Benedict* also prescribed a Gospel Canticle after the Lesson at [Vespers](/glossary/liturgical-terms#vespers),[^69] and Amalarius attested to the use of the *Magnificat* as this canticle.[^70] Accordingly, the Prayer Book places the *Magnificat* after the First Lesson at Evening Prayer.[^71] As with the *Benedictus*, this placement is fitting because, like [Our Lady](/people/saints/biblical/blessed-virgin-mary)—who first proclaimed the words of this canticle—it links together the Old and New Testaments.

According to ancient precedent, the [O Antiphons](/liturgy/liturgical-year/seasons/advent/o-antiphons) are sung before and after the *Magnificat* at Vespers the last eight nights of Advent.[^72] Accordingly, this Office uses the verses of the English paraphrase—[*O Come, O Come, Emmanuel*](/liturgy/music/hymns/o-come-o-come-emmanuel)—as antiphons before and after the *Magnificat* at Evening Prayer on these nights. Five of the verses are drawn from [*Hymns Ancient & Modern*](/glossary/liturgical-terms#hymns-ancient--modern) (1861).[^73] The verses for [*O Sapientia*](/liturgy/music/chants/o-sapientia) and [*O Rex gentium*](/liturgy/music/chants/o-rex-gentium)—which were paraphrased later—are taken from *The Hymnal of the Episcopal Church* (1940).[^74] The paraphrase of [*O Virgo virginum*](/liturgy/music/chants/o-virgo-virginum)—the eighth O Antiphon according to the medieval English use—is unique to this Office. The verses are sung to the traditional melody, the earliest surviving manuscripts of which date back to the 15th century.[^75]

[***Nunc dimittis***](/liturgy/music/chants/nunc-dimittis)

The *Nunc dimittis* has been used as an evening prayer since at least the 4th century, when it was included in the *Apostolic Constitutions*.[^76] Amalarius attested to its use in the Office after the Lesson at Compline.[^77] Accordingly, the Prayer Book places the *Nunc dimittis* after the Second Lesson at Evening Prayer.[^78] This placement is fitting because, like Simeon—who, when first reciting this canticle, beheld the face of the child King—we end the day beholding the Lord in his incarnate glory.

As an optional devotional practice, those comfortable singing in Latin may follow the Benedictine practice of singing the *Nunc dimittis* on Lord's Days with the antiphon [*Salva nos Domine*](/liturgy/music/chants/salva-nos-domine) sung before and after.[^79]

## Supplementary Third Lesson

After the Gospel Canticles, the Psalter allows for a supplementary Third Lesson for the commemoration of saints.[^80] The lessons for the commemoration of saints in this Office are drawn from the 1897 edition of [*Lives of the Saints*](/glossary/liturgical-terms#lives-of-the-saints).[^81] The Third Lesson is read in the same manner as the other Lessons. Before reading, the lector says, "Here beginneth a reading on the life of *N*."

## Concluding Rites
---

## Apostles' Creed

The Concluding Rites of the Office begin with the [Apostles' Creed](/liturgy/creeds/apostles-creed).[^82] The Apostles' Creed is one of the most enduring and widely used Christian confessions of faith, tracing its roots back to as early as the 2nd century. It developed from the Old Roman Creed, reaching its present form by the 8th century.[^83] The recitation of the Apostles' Creed at Morning and Evening Prayer reflects its ancient use at Prime and Compline.[^84] During the Creed, all stand facing the altar, the Officiant standing *junctis manibus*. At the Name of Jesus, all bow the head. At the conclusion of the Creed, all make the Sign of the Cross.[^85]

## Salutation

After the Apostles' Creed, the choir returns to its lateral position, and the priest or deacon turns to the congregation and recites the [Salutation](/glossary/liturgical-terms#salutation), first extending and then joining his hands.[^86][^87] According to the Psalter, if the Officiant is not a priest or a deacon, the following may be recited instead:

<Poetry>
|  \V O Lord, hear our prayer.
|  \R And let our cry come unto thee.
|  \V Let us pray.[^88]
</Poetry>

## Suffrages

Immediately following the Salutation, all kneel for the recitation of the [Suffrages](/glossary/liturgical-terms#suffrages).[^89] If the Officiant is a priest or a deacon, he may continue to stand.[^90] The Suffrages are intercessions meant to serve as an active response to [St. Paul's](/people/saints/apostles/paul) exhortation in his First Epistle to Timothy: "I urge that supplications, prayers, intercessions, and thanksgivings be made for all people, for kings and all who are in high positions, that we may lead a peaceful and quiet life, godly and dignified in every way. This is good, and it is pleasing in the sight of God our Savior, who desires all people to be saved and to come to the knowledge of the truth" (1 Tim. 2:1-4). The Suffrages and Collects at the end of the Office trace their roots back to the Use of Sarum.[^91]

## Collects

After the Suffrages come the Collects, which are chanted by the Officiant.[^92] According to the Psalter, the Collects are chanted either using the [festal tone](/liturgy/daily-office/chant-rubrics#the-collects) or, on [ferial](/glossary/liturgical-terms#ferial) days, *recto tono*.[^93] The collects of the day are chanted first, followed by the collects belonging to the Ordinary: *For Peace* and *For Grace* at Morning Prayer and *For Peace* and *For Aid Against Perils* at Evening Prayer.[^94]

The collects for this Office are drawn principally from the Prayer Book.[^95] When the Prayer Book does not provide one, the appointed collect is drawn from the 1880 edition of the English [diurnal](/glossary/liturgical-terms#diurnal) [*Breviary Offices*](/glossary/liturgical-terms#breviary-offices).[^96] Collects for the commemoration of saints are drawn from the 2024 edition of [*Lesser Feasts and Fasts*](/glossary/liturgical-terms#lesser-feasts-and-fasts).[^97]

## Supplementary Prayers

After the Collects at Evening Prayer, the Prayer Book includes a rubric stating, "In places where it may be convenient, here followeth the <Definition lang="greek" anchor="antiphona" text="Anthem" />."[^98] Many scholars believe this refers to the seasonal Marian Antiphons, which were sung at the end of Compline in the medieval Roman Office. These antiphons can be found in the Psalter.[^99] The Prayer Book also contains supplementary prayers which the Officiant may recite before the Grace at his discretion, including *A Prayer for Civil Authorities* and *A Prayer for Clergy and People*.[^100]

## The Grace

Finally, the Grace (2 Cor. 13:14)—[St. Paul's](/people/saints/apostles/paul) benediction at the end of his Second Epistle to the Corinthians—is recited by the Officiant to conclude the Office.[^101] The Grace serves as a prayer for the gifts of the Three Persons of the Trinity, and has been used as a closing doxology for the Office since 1662.[^102]

The Office thus begins with repentance, moves through the singing and hearing of God's Word, and ends with a petition for divine favor in the words of the Apostle. The Sign of the Cross is made at the Grace, marking the conclusion of the Office and serving as a visible prayer for God's continuing presence.[^103]

## After the Office
---

According to the *Rule of Saint Benedict*, whatever time remains after Morning Prayer is to be devoted to thoughtful study of the Psalms and Lessons.[^104] It is also appropriate to include here the study of hymns, the Church Fathers, and the lives of the saints.
