<Initial text="Daily Office: Ordinary" />

The <Definition lang="latin" anchor="ordinarius" text="Ordinary" /> sets forth the fixed order of the Daily Office together with the customary manner in which it is celebrated.

### <Large size="xl" text="Entrance" />

<Red large={true} text="†" /> The quiet time before the Office is for silent prayer and the lighting of the candles. At the sound of the bell, all rise as the ministers and choir enter.[^1] They proceed to the [chancel](/glossary/liturgical-terms#chancel), bow toward the altar, and take their [appointed places](/glossary/liturgical-terms#decani).[^2] The ministers and choir remain standing in their normal lateral position.<sup>[a]</sup>

<ol type="a" style={{ margin: "1.5rem 0 2rem", fontSize: "0.9375rem" }}>
  <li>The Sarum Customary directed the choir to face the altar.[^4] The 1552 Prayer Book states that “the Minister shall so turn him, as the people may best hear” (BCP 1552, 96). This appears to have displaced the eastward orientation, a conclusion consistent with later English usage, as observed at Canterbury, York, and Westminster.</li>
</ol>

---

### <Large size="xl" text="Penitence" />

## <Large text="Sentences" />

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text="❡ The Minister shall begin the [Office] by reading one or more of the following Sentences of Scripture (BCP 1928, 3)." />
</div>

<Red large={true} text="†" /> The Officiant reads one or more of the Sentences from his place in the choir.<sup>[a]</sup> Any of the Sentences may be read at the Officiant’s discretion.[^3]

<Grey text="※" /> The Sentences are meant to furnish "sundry places" in which the Scripture moveth us to confess our sins before Almighty God with "an humble, lowly, penitent, and obedient heart; to the end that we may obtain forgiveness of the same, by his infinite goodness and mercy."

<ol type="a" style={{ margin: "1.5rem 0 2rem", fontSize: "0.9375rem" }}>
  <li>The 1549 Prayer Book begins the Office with “the Priest being in the Quire.” Canon 14 of the Canons of the Church of England (1603) permits the Divine Service to be conducted in such part of the church as the bishop deems most suitable for the edification of the people.[^5]</li>
</ol>

## <Large text="Exhortation" />

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text='❡ Then the Minister shall say, "Dearly beloved brethren..." (BCP 1928, 5).' />
</div>

<Grey text="※" /> The Exhortation outlines the theology of the Office: that we are to acknowledge our sins before God, set forth his most worthy praise, hear his most holy Word, and "ask those things which are requisite and necessary, as well for the body as the soul."

## <Large text="General Confession" />

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text="❡ To be said by the whole Congregation, after the Minister, all kneeling (BCP 1928, 6)." />
</div>

<Red large={true} text="†" /> The General Confession is recited by the Officiant and congregation in unison.<sup>[a]</sup> The *<Definition lang="hebrew" anchor="amen" text="Amen" />* is recited by all and therefore is not italicized. When the Prayer Book italicizes *Amen*, the Officiant is to pause while the people respond, “Amen.”[^6]

<Grey text="※" /> The General Confession is an act of repentance in which we "with confidence draw near to the throne of grace, that we may receive mercy and find grace to help in time of need" (Heb. 4:16). The 1530 [*Augsburg Confession*](/meditations/luther-on-repentance) describes repentance as consisting of two parts: contrition and faith.[^7] Reciting the General Confession at Morning and Evening Prayer reflects the ancient practice of twice-daily confession at [Prime](/glossary/liturgical-terms#prime) and [Compline](/glossary/liturgical-terms#compline).[^8]

<ol type="a" style={{ margin: "1.5rem 0 2rem", fontSize: "0.9375rem" }}>
  <li>Some commentators interpret "after the Minister" to mean that the Officiant recites each clause, pausing while the people repeat it.[^9] Others interpret it simply to mean "with the Minister."[^10] This customary adopts the latter practice.</li>
</ol>

## <Large text="Absolution" />

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text="❡ To be made by the Priest alone, standing; the People still kneeling (BCP 1928, 7)." />
  <Rubric text="❡ If no priest be present the person saying the Service shall read the Collect for the Twenty-First Sunday after Trinity, that person and the people still kneeling (BCP 1662, 4; Measure 1968)." />
</div>

### <Large size="xl" text="Opening" />

## <Large text="Lord's Prayer" />

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text="❡ Then the Minister shall kneel, and say the Lord's Prayer; the People still kneeling, and repeating it with him (BCP 1928, 7)." />
</div>

<Grey text="※" /> The Sarum Use prescribed the silent recitation of the [Lord's Prayer](/liturgy/prayers/our-father) and [Hail Mary](/liturgy/prayers/hail-mary) before the beginning of the Office. The 1549 Prayer Book omitted the Hail Mary and directed that the Officiant say the Lord's Prayer "with a loud voice." The 1662 Prayer Book directed that it be said by all.[^11]

The Lord's Prayer naturally follows the Absolution, for it is through the assurance of forgiveness in Christ that we find the confidence to address God as our heavenly Father. As the burning coal purified Isaiah's lips, so the Absolution cleanses us and prepares us to respond, "Here I am!" (Isa. 6:6-8), to the glory of God's holy Name.

## <Large text="Versicles" />

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text='❡ Then likewise he shall say, "O Lord, open thou our lips..." \a (BCP 1928, 7).' />
  <Rubric text='❡ Here, all standing up, the Minister shall say, "Glory be..." (BCP 1928, 8).' />
</div>

<Red large={true} text="†" /> After reciting the first half of the Opening Versicles, all stand and bow at the *Gloria Patri*, rising at the words “As it was in the beginning.” The same practice is observed any time the *Gloria Patri* is recited in the Office.<sup>[b]</sup>

<div style={{ margin: "1.5rem 0" }}>
  <p style={{ marginBottom: "0.5rem" }}>Omitted</p>
  <ul style={{ marginTop: 0 }}>
    <li>Last three days of Holy Week and All Souls' Day[^12]</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<Grey text="※" /> The 6th-century [*Rule of Saint Benedict*](/meditations/the-rule-of-saint-benedict) (RSB) prescribed two [versicles](/glossary/liturgical-terms#versicle) for the opening of [Matins](/glossary/liturgical-terms#matins): the [*Domine labia mea*](/liturgy/music/chants/domine-labia-mea) and the [*Deus in adjutorium*](/liturgy/music/chants/deus-in-adjutorium). This was to be followed by the [*Gloria Patri*](/liturgy/music/chants/gloria-patri) and the [*Alleluia*](/liturgy/music/chants/alleluia). The *Domine labia mea* was to be omitted the other hours of the day, and the *Alleluia* was to be omitted during certain times of the year.[^13] The Prayer Book simplified these rubrics by appointing the *Domine labia mea* for both Morning and Evening Prayer—as it flows more naturally into the praise of the *Gloria Patri* and the Invitatory—and by replacing the *Alleluia* with its English equivalent, "Praise ye the Lord," to be used throughout the year. It also added the response, "The Lord's Name be praised," thus allowing the entire opening to be chanted <Definition lang="english" anchor="antiphonal" text="antiphonally" />.[^14]

<ol type="a" style={{ margin: "1.5rem 0 2rem", fontSize: "0.9375rem" }}>
  <li>The old Roman Breviary directs that the [Sign of the Cross](/liturgy/prayers/sign-of-the-cross) be made on the lips with the thumb of the right hand at the *Domine labia mea*.[^15] While this practice seems to have been adopted in some places, this customary does not prescribe it.</li>
  <li>The Sarum Customary directed the choir to stand and face the altar at the *Gloria Patri*.[^16] The York Ordinal further directed that a bow be made until the words “As it was in the beginning,” out of reverence for the Holy Trinity.[^17] Although the eastward orientation was not retained in later English usage, the standing was retained.[^18] The bow was likewise retained in some places, chiefly by the ministers, as observed at Canterbury and Westminster.</li>
</ol>

### <Large size="xl" text="Psalmody" />

## <Large text="Invitatory" />

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text="❡ Then shall be said or sung the following Canticle; except on those days for which other Canticles are appointed; \a and except also, that Psalm 95 may be used in this place \b (BCP 1928, 8)." />
  <Rubric text="❡ On Ash Wednesday and Good Friday the Venite may be omitted \c (BCP 1928, 8)." />
  <Rubric text='❡ On the days hereafter named, immediately before the Venite may be sung or said, On the Sundays in Advent. Our King and Saviour...\d (BCP 1928, 8).' />
</div>

<Red large={true} text="†" /> At Morning Prayer, all remain standing for the [*Venite*](/liturgy/music/chants/venite).<sup>[e]</sup> The [Precentor](/glossary/liturgical-terms#precentor) begins by singing the [antiphon](/glossary/liturgical-terms#antiphon), and the choir repeats it. Then the Precentor sings the verses, and the choir sings the antiphon at the appointed places, including after the *Gloria Patri*.[^19] Each of the provided antiphons is used for the entire season during which it is appointed, rather than exclusively on its feast day, Sunday, or [octave](/glossary/liturgical-terms#octave). In this way, the *Venite* is always chanted with an antiphon.

<div style={{ margin: "1.5rem 0" }}>
  <p style={{ marginBottom: "0.5rem" }}>Omitted</p>
  <ul style={{ marginTop: 0 }}>
    <li>Octave of Easter, [*Pascha nostrum*](/liturgy/music/chants/pascha-nostrum) sung instead (BCP 1928, 162)</li>
    <li>Last three days of Holy Week and All Souls' Day[^20]</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<Grey text="※" /> As the name implies, the [Invitatory](/glossary/liturgical-terms#invitatory) serves as a calling or invitation to praise. The RSB prescribed the *Venite* (Psalm 95) as the Invitatory for Matins and directed that it be sung with an antiphon.[^21] The exact manner of singing the Invitatory antiphon has varied over the centuries. The 15th-century *Myroure of Our Ladye* states that it is sung five times in full during the *Venite*, corresponding to the five senses, which those who serve the Lord must diligently guard.[^22] Accordingly, the [*Saint Dunstan's Psalter*](/glossary/liturgical-terms#saint-dunstans-plainsong-psalter) (SDP) marks five places in the *Venite* where the antiphon is to be recited.[^23] The last two verses of the *Venite* in the Prayer Book are drawn from Psalm 96:9 and Psalm 96:13. They emphasize the beauty of Christ and the Glory of his coming.

<ol type="a" style={{ margin: "1.5rem 0 2rem", fontSize: "0.9375rem" }}>
  <li>The "Canticle" here refers to Psalm 95, and "other Canticles" refers to the *Pascha nostrum*.</li>
  <li>This refers to when Psalm 95 shows up in the psalms of the day. In this case, rather than omitting the Invitatory, this customary omits Psalm 95 from the psalms of the day.[^24]</li>
  <li>This Office retains the *Venite* on Ash Wednesday according to ancient precedent.[^25]</li>
  <li>The Prayer Book supplies antiphons for all the seasons of the liturgical year except for Lent. The Lenten antiphon can be found in the SDP.[^26]</li>
  <li>The Sarum Customary directed the choir to stand for the entirety of the Office, sitting only for the Lessons.[^27] This practice was largely retained in later English usage.[^28]</li>
</ol>

## <Large text="Psalms" />

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text="❡ Then shall follow a Portion of the Psalms, according to the Use of this Church. And at the end of every Psalm, and likewise at the end of the [Canticles], shall be sung or said the Gloria Patri (BCP 1928, 9)." />
</div>

<Red large={true} text="†" /> The people sit while the choir remains standing for the Psalms.<sup>[a]</sup> With the exception of the *Benedicite*, every psalm and canticle is concluded with the *Gloria Patri*.

<Grey text="※" /> Antiphonal psalmody traces its origins to the Jewish temple, and the early Christians continued on in the regular chanting of psalms. The RSB outlined a seven-day psalter, so that by the end of each week all 150 Psalms would have been chanted—a task, [St. Benedict](/people/saints/medieval/benedict-of-nursia) notes, that his predecessors had accomplished within a single day.[^29] In order to make the Office more accessible to the laity, the Prayer Book adopted a monthly psalter.[^30]

<ol type="a" style={{ margin: "1.5rem 0 2rem", fontSize: "0.9375rem" }}>
  <li>At the time of this writing, this is the observed practice at Canterbury, York, and Westminster.</li>
</ol>

---

## <Large size="xl" text="First Lesson" />

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text="❡ Then shall be read the First Lesson, according to the Table or Calendar.\a And Note, That before every Lesson, the Minister shall say, Here beginneth such a Chapter (or Verse of such a Chapter) of such a Book; and after every Lesson, Here endeth the First (or Second) Lesson (BCP 1928, 9)." />
</div>

<Red large={true} text="†" /> All sit for the First Lesson from the Old Testament.<sup>[b]</sup> The Reader stands and approaches the [lectern](/glossary/liturgical-terms#lectern). Before reading, he says, "The *nth* chapter of the Book of *N*, beginning at the *nth* verse." After reading, he says, "Here endeth the *Nth* Lesson."

<ol type="a" style={{ margin: "1.5rem 0 2rem", fontSize: "0.9375rem" }}>
  <li>The lessons for the Office follow the Prayer Book's one-year [lectionary](/glossary/liturgical-terms#lectionary) and therefore may not always correspond precisely to the feast or Sunday. The Epistle and Gospel appointed for the feast or Sunday are read at Holy Communion. For simplicity, canticles may be both read and sung in the same Office, provided they are neither read twice nor sung twice.</li>
  <li>The RSB prescribed sitting for the Lessons at Matins,[^31] a practice that was retained in later English usage.[^32]</li>
</ol>

### <Large size="xl" text="Canticle" />

## <Large text="Te Deum" />

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text="❡ Here shall be said or sung the following Hymn (BCP 1928, 10)." />
</div>

<Red large={true} text="†" /> At Morning Prayer, all stand for the [*Te Deum*](/liturgy/music/chants/te-deum).

<div style={{ margin: "1.5rem 0" }}>
  <p style={{ marginBottom: "0.5rem" }}>Daily</p>
  <ul style={{ marginTop: 0 }}>
    <li>All Feasts and Lord's Days</li>
    <li>Octave of Epiphany</li>
    <li>Eastertide, except on Rogation Days</li>
    <li>Whitsuntide, except on Ember Days</li>
  </ul>
  <p style={{ marginBottom: "0.5rem" }}>Omitted</p>
  <ul style={{ marginTop: 0 }}>
    <li>Advent</li>
    <li>The Holy Innocents, unless it falls on a Sunday</li>
    <li>From Septuagesima to Easter (exclusive)</li>
    <li>All Souls' Day[^33][^34]</li>
  </ul>
</div>

<Grey text="※" /> The *Te Deum* is an ancient hymn. Some manuscripts attribute it to [St. Ambrose](/people/saints/latin-fathers/ambrose-of-milan) and [St. Augustine](/people/saints/latin-fathers/augustine-of-hippo), giving rise to the belief that it was composed for Augustine's baptism in 386 AD. More likely, however, their names became associated with the hymn because they helped introduce it into the Western Church. Scholars generally agree that, in its present form, the hymn was composed around the 4th century.[^35] The RSB prescribed the *Te Deum* for Matins on Lord's Days.[^36]

## <Large text="Benedicite" />

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text="❡ Or this Canticle (BCP 1928, 11)." />
</div>

<Red large={true} text="†" /> At Morning Prayer, when the *Te Deum* is not sung, all stand for the [*Benedicite*](/liturgy/music/chants/benedicite). The *Gloria Patri* is not recited at the end of the *Benedicite*, as its last verse forms the [doxology](/glossary/liturgical-terms#doxology). As with the *Gloria Patri*, all bow at the doxology, rising at the words “praise and magnify him.”<sup>[a]</sup>

<Grey text="※" /> The words of the *Benedicite* come from the *Prayer of Azariah and Song of the Three Holy Children*, which is found in the [Septuagint](/bible/translations/septuagint) version of Daniel 3 and was included in the [Vulgate](/bible/translations/vulgate). The *Benedicite* belongs to the ancient weekly cycle of [Old Testament Canticles](/liturgy/daily-office/psalter#old-testament-canticles) recited at [Lauds](/glossary/liturgical-terms#lauds), where it was appointed for Lord's Days.[^37]

<ol type="a" style={{ margin: "1.5rem 0 2rem", fontSize: "0.9375rem" }}>
  <li>The 1549 Prayer Book replaced the last two verses of the *Benedicite*, which form the canticle’s doxology, with the *Gloria Patri*. In 1928, the first of these verses, “Let us bless the Father,” was restored, but the second, together with the verse, “O Ananias, Azarias, and Misael,” was omitted.[^38] The York Ordinal expressly states to bow at the penultimate verse of the *Benedicite*, that is, at "Let us bless the Father."[^39]</li>
</ol>

## <Large text="Magnificat" />

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text="❡ After which shall be sung or said the Hymn called Magnificat (BCP 1928, 26)." />
</div>

<Red large={true} text="†" /> At Evening Prayer, all stand for the [*Magnificat*](/liturgy/music/chants/magnificat). As a feature of this customary, the verses of [*O Come, O Come, Emmanuel*](/liturgy/music/hymns/o-come-o-come-emmanuel) are distributed and sung in place of the [O Antiphons](/liturgy/liturgical-year/seasons/advent/o-antiphons) during the last eight nights of Advent.<sup>[a]</sup>

<Grey text="※" /> The RSB prescribed a Gospel Canticle after the Lesson at [Vespers](/glossary/liturgical-terms#vespers).[^40] In 820 AD, Amalarius, Frankish bishop and contemporary of Charlemagne, attested to the use of the *Magnificat* as this canticle.[^41] Accordingly, the Prayer Book places the *Magnificat* after the First Lesson at Evening Prayer. This placement is fitting because, like [Our Lady](/people/saints/biblical/blessed-virgin-mary)—who first proclaimed the words of this canticle—it links together the Old and New Testaments. The O Antiphons have long accompanied the Magnificat during the last eight nights of Advent.[^42]

<ol type="a" style={{ margin: "1.5rem 0 2rem", fontSize: "0.9375rem" }}>
  <li>Five of the verses of *O Come, O Come, Emmanuel* are drawn from [*Hymns Ancient & Modern*](/glossary/liturgical-terms#hymns-ancient--modern) (1861).[^43] The verses for [*O Sapientia*](/liturgy/music/chants/o-sapientia) and [*O Rex gentium*](/liturgy/music/chants/o-rex-gentium)—which were paraphrased later—are taken from *The Hymnal of the Episcopal Church* (1940).[^44] The paraphrase of [*O Virgo virginum*](/liturgy/music/chants/o-virgo-virginum)—the eighth O Antiphon according to the medieval English use—is unique to this Office. The verses are sung to the traditional melody, the earliest surviving manuscripts of which date back to the 15th century.[^45]</li>
</ol>

### <Large size="xl" text="Canticle" />

## <Large text="Benedictus" />

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text="❡ And after that shall be sung or said the Hymn following (BCP 1928, 14)." />
</div>

<Red large={true} text="†" /> At Morning Prayer, all stand for the [*Benedictus*](/liturgy/music/chants/benedictus).

<Grey text="※" /> The RSB prescribed a Gospel Canticle after the Lessons at Lauds.[^46] In 820 AD, Amalarius attested to the use of the *Benedictus* as this canticle.[^47] Accordingly, the Prayer Book places the *Benedictus* after the Second Lesson at Morning Prayer. This placement is fitting because, like [St. John the Baptist](/people/saints/biblical/john-the-baptist)—about whom this canticle is written—it links together the Old and New Testaments.

## <Large text="Nunc dimittis" />

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text="❡ And after that shall be sung or said the Hymn called Nunc dimittis (BCP 1928, 28)." />
</div>

<Red large={true} text="†" /> At Evening Prayer, all stand for the [*Nunc dimittis*](/liturgy/music/chants/nunc-dimittis).

<Grey text="※" /> The *Nunc dimittis* has been used as an evening prayer since at least the 4th century, when it was included in the *Apostolic Constitutions*.[^48] In 820 AD, Amalarius attested to its use at Compline.[^49] Accordingly, the Prayer Book places the *Nunc dimittis* after the Second Lesson at Evening Prayer. This placement is fitting because, like Simeon—who, when first reciting this canticle, beheld the face of the child King—we end the day beholding the Lord in his incarnate glory.

## <Large size="xl" text="Apostles' Creed" />

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text="❡ Then shall be said the Apostles' Creed by the Minister and the people, standing (BCP 1928, 15)." />
</div>

<Red large={true} text="†" /> All stand facing the altar for the [Apostles' Creed](/liturgy/creeds/apostles-creed).<sup>[a]</sup> It is customary to bow the head at the Name of Jesus.<sup>[b]</sup>

<Grey text="※" /> The Apostles' Creed is one of the most enduring and widely used Christian confessions of faith, tracing its roots back to as early as the 2nd century. It developed from the Old Roman Creed, reaching its present form by the 8th century.[^50] The Sarum Use prescribed the silent recitation of the Creed among the Suffrages at Prime and Compline. The 1549 Book of Common Prayer directed that the Officiant recite the Creed “with a loud voice.” The 1552 Prayer Book directed that it be said by all.[^51]

<ol type="a" style={{ margin: "1.5rem 0 2rem", fontSize: "0.9375rem" }}>
  <li>It has been customary to stand facing the altar for the Apostles' Creed in the Daily Office since at least the 17th century. This practice reflects the ancient Christian custom of praying toward the East, and the baptismal rite in which catechumens renounce the devil facing West before turning East to profess their faith in Christ.[^52]</li>
  <li>Bowing the head at the Name of Jesus stems from Canon 18 of the Canons of the Church of England (1603), which says, “When in time of Divine Service the Lord Jesus shall be mentioned, due and lowly reverence shall be done by all persons present, as it hath been accustomed.”[^53]</li>
</ol>

### <Large size="xl" text="Prayers" />

## <Large text="Salutation" />

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text='❡ The Minister first pronouncing, "The Lord be with you..." (BCP 1928, 16).' />
</div>

<Red large={true} text="†" /> The choir returns to its lateral position,<sup>[a]</sup> and the priest or deacon recites the [Salutation](/glossary/liturgical-terms#salutation). If the Officiant is not a priest or deacon, he instead recites, ["O Lord, hear our prayer."](/glossary/liturgical-terms#o-lord-hear-my-prayer)<sup>[b]</sup>

<Grey text="※" /> The Salutation has been pronounced by ordained men since ancient times. It is commonly used to introduce formal prayers. Its origin is scriptural (Ruth 2:4), and many believe it to be apostolic.[^54] The versicle is a blessing directed to the congregation, and the response is a prayer that the Lord, by the Holy Spirit, would bless the priest or deacon in his vocation. The alternative, "O Lord, hear our prayer," is also ancient and is taken from Psalm 102:1. It is used in many places throughout the Prayer Book (BCP 1928, 62).

<ol type="a" style={{ margin: "1.5rem 0 2rem", fontSize: "0.9375rem" }}>
  <li>At the time of this writing, this is the observed practice at Canterbury, York, and Westminster.</li>
  <li>The rubric to substitute “O Lord, hear my prayer” when the Officiant is not a priest or deacon comes from the old Roman Breviary.[^55]</li>
</ol>

## <Large text="Suffrages" />

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text="❡ And after that, these Prayers following, the people devoutly kneeling (BCP 1928, 16)." />
</div>

<Red large={true} text="†" /> The people kneel for the [Suffrages](/glossary/liturgical-terms#suffrages). The Officiant and the choir remain standing.<sup>[a]</sup>

<Grey text="※" /> The Suffrages are intercessions meant to serve as an active response to [St. Paul's](/people/saints/apostles/paul) exhortation from his First Epistle to Timothy: "I urge that supplications, prayers, intercessions, and thanksgivings be made for all people, for kings and all who are in high positions, that we may lead a peaceful and quiet life, godly and dignified in every way. This is good, and it is pleasing in the sight of God our Savior, who desires all people to be saved and to come to the knowledge of the truth" (1 Tim. 2:1-4).

<ol type="a" style={{ margin: "1.5rem 0 2rem", fontSize: "0.9375rem" }}>
  <li>This reflects the practice of the pre-Reformation Office.[^56]</li>
</ol>

## <Large text="Collects" />

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text="Then shall be said the Collect for the Day, and after that the Collects (BCP 1928, 31)." />
</div>

<Red large={true} text="†" /> The Officiant chants the [Collects](/glossary/liturgical-terms#collect). The Collects are chanted either (1) using the [festal tone](/liturgy/daily-office/chant-rubrics#the-collects), or (2) *<Definition lang="latin" anchor="recto-tono" text="recto tono" />* on [ferial](/glossary/liturgical-terms#ferial) days.[^57] The Collects of the Day are chanted first,<sup>[a]</sup> followed by the Collects belonging to the Ordinary.<sup>[b]</sup>

<Grey text="※" /> The RSB prescribed that the Day Hours end with the "dismissals."<sup>[c]</sup> The nature of these "dismissals" is unclear, but in 820 AD, Amalarius attested to the use of the Suffrages and Collect at the end of the Office.[^58] The Suffrages and Collect likewise appear at the end of the Office in the Sarum Use.

<ol type="a" style={{ margin: "1.5rem 0 2rem", fontSize: "0.9375rem" }}>
  <li>The Collects of the Day are drawn principally from the Prayer Book (BCP 1928, 90). When the Prayer Book does not provide one, the appointed Collect is drawn from the 1880 edition of [*Breviary Offices*](/glossary/liturgical-terms#breviary-offices).[^59] Collects for the commemoration of saints are drawn from the 2024 edition of [*Lesser Feasts and Fasts*](/glossary/liturgical-terms#lesser-feasts-and-fasts).[^60]</li>
  <li>The Collects belonging to the Ordinary are *For Pace* and *For Grace* at Morning Prayer, and *For Peace* and *For Aid Against Perils* at Evening Prayer.</li>
  <li>Some translators use the word "Collect", but the Latin is *<Definition lang="latin" anchor="missae" text="missae" />*, not *<Definition lang="latin" anchor="oratio" text="oratio" />*.[^61]</li>
</ol>

### <Large size="xl" text="Anthem" />

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text="❡ In places where it may be convenient, here followeth the Anthem (BCP 1928, 31)." />
</div>

<Red large={true} text="†" /> At Evening Prayer, an <Definition lang="greek" anchor="antiphona" text="Anthem" /> may be sung. The Officiant may sit during the Anthem.<sup>[a]</sup>

<Grey text="※" /> The 1549 Prayer Book concluded the Office with the Collects (BCP 1549, 7). The 1662 Prayer Book introduced a rubric to allow for the singing of an Anthem, which many scholars believe corresponds to the antiphon used at the end of the pre-Reformation Office. The 1789 American Prayer Book did not include this rubric, but it was reintroduced for Evening Prayer in 1892.[^62]

<ol type="a" style={{ margin: "1.5rem 0 2rem", fontSize: "0.9375rem" }}>
  <li>At the time of this writing, this is the observed practice at Canterbury, York, and Westminster.</li>
</ol>

### <Large size="xl" text="Conclusion" />

## <Large text="Closing Prayers" />

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text="❡ The Minister may here end the [Office] with such Prayer, or Prayers, taken out of this Book, as he shall think fit (BCP 1928, 31)." />
</div>

<Red large={true} text="†" /> One or more Closing Prayers may be recited at the discretion of the Officiant. If so, the Officiant remains standing, and the choir kneels with the people for the remainder of the Office. If the Closing Prayers are omitted, the choir remains standing.<sup>[a]</sup>

<Grey text="※" /> Along with the Anthem rubric, the 1662 Prayer book introduced a series of Closing Prayers at the end of the Office, such as *A Prayer, for the King's Majesty*, *A Prayer for the Royal Family*, and *A Prayer for the Clergy and People* (BCP 1662, 14). The 1789 American Prayer Book replaced the prayers for the King and Royal Family with *A Prayer for The President of the United States, and all in Civil Authority*.[^63] The 1928 Prayer Book left the use of these prayers to the discretion of the Officiant.

<ol type="a" style={{ margin: "1.5rem 0 2rem", fontSize: "0.9375rem" }}>
  <li>The Sarum Customary states that the choir is to remain standing until the end of the Office. Later English use appoints that the choir kneel for the Closing Prayers, however, it doesn't make sense to kneel if the Office is concluding with the Grace only.</li>
</ol>

## <Large text="The Grace" />

<div style={{ marginBottom: "1.5rem" }}>
  <Rubric text="❡ The Minister may here end the [Office] with such general intercessions taken out of this Book, as he shall think fit, or with the Grace (BCP 1928, 17)." />
</div>

<Red large={true} text="†" /> The Officiant recites the Grace (2 Cor. 13:14) to conclude the Office.

<Grey text="※" /> The RSB prescribed that Matins and Compline conclude with a benediction.[^64] The 1662 Prayer Book appointed the Grace—the ancient benediction of St. Paul from his Second Epistle to the Corinthians—for the conclusion of the Office (BCP 1662, 15). As the name implies, the Grace serves as a prayer for "the grace of our Lord Jesus Christ, and the love of God, and the fellowship of the Holy Ghost" (2 Cor. 13:14). The Office thus begins with repentance, moves through the singing and hearing of God's Word, and ends with a petition for divine favor in the words of the Apostle.

## <Large size="xl" text="Departure" />

<Red large={true} text="†" /> All rise. The ministers and choir bow toward the altar and proceed to the [sacristy](/glossary/liturgical-terms#sacristy).[^65] According to the RSB, whatever time remains after Morning Prayer is to be devoted to thoughtful study of the Psalms and Lessons.[^66] It is also appropriate to include here the study of hymns, the Church Fathers, and the lives of the saints.
