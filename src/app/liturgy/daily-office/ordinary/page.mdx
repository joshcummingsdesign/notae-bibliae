<Initial text="Daily Office: Ordinary" />

The <Definition lang="latin" anchor="ordinarius" text="Ordinary" /> sets forth the fixed order of the Daily Office along with the customary manner in which it is celebrated.

## Before the Office
---

## Ceremonial

This Office commends the use of the [cassock](/liturgy/vesture/clerical-dress#cassock) and [surplice](/liturgy/vesture/vestments#surplice) as the customary vesture of the ministers.[^1] It is likewise customary that two altar candles be lighted.[^2]

## Silent Prayer

It is common practice to kneel in silent prayer before the Office. The 15th-century [*Myroure of Our Ladye*](/meditations/silent-prayer-before-the-daily-office), written for the Bridgettine Sisters of Syon Abbey in West London, beautifully describes in detail the purpose of kneeling in silent prayer as a way of preparing one's heart and mind for the Office.[^3]

## Introductory Rites
---

## Sentences

The ministers proceed from the [sacristy](/glossary/liturgical-terms#sacristy) to the [chancel](/glossary/liturgical-terms#chancel), at which time all stand. They take their [appointed places](/glossary/liturgical-terms#decani) in church, and the Officiant turns to the congregation and reads one or more of the Sentences.[^4]

❦ In the words of the Exhortation, which immediately follows, the Sentences are meant to furnish “sundry places” in which the Scripture moves us to confess our sins before Almighty God with a “humble, lowly, penitent, and obedient heart; to the end that we may obtain forgiveness of the same, by his infinite goodness and mercy.”[^5]

<small>*<Red text="+" /> Although the Prayer Book indicates the appropriate seasons for the Sentences, any of them may be used at the Minister's discretion.[^6]*</small>

## Exhortation

The Officiant recites the Exhortation. At the words, "accompany me with a pure heart," all kneel. Alternatively, the shorter Bidding may be recited if the congregation is already familiar with the theology of the Office. In this case, all kneel after the Bidding.

❦ The 1892 American Prayer Book introduced the shorter Bidding for Evening Prayer. The 1928 Prayer Book extended its use to Morning Prayer.[^7]

## General Confession

All recite the General Confession kneeling.[^8]

❦ The General Confession is an act of repentance in which we "with confidence draw near to the throne of grace, that we may receive mercy and find grace to help in time of need" (Heb. 4:16). The 1530 [*Augsburg Confession*](/meditations/luther-on-repentance) beautifully describes in detail the meaning and purpose of repentance.[^9] Reciting the General Confession at Morning and Evening Prayer reflects the ancient practice of twice-daily confession at [Prime](/glossary/liturgical-terms#prime) and [Compline](/glossary/liturgical-terms#compline).[^10]

<small>*<Red text="+" /> Note that here, as in all other instances, the <Definition lang="hebrew" anchor="amen" text="Amen" /> is recited by the entire congregation. Because it forms part of a text recited by all, it is not italicized. When the Prayer Book italicizes *Amen*, it signifies the congregation's response to a prayer said by the Officiant.[^11]*</small>

## Absolution

The priest stands and faces the congregation to pronounce the Absolution.[^12] The [Sign of the Cross](/liturgy/prayers/sign-of-the-cross) is made at the words, "He pardoneth and absolveth."[^13] At Evening Prayer, the priest may use the shorter alternative Absolution, the Sign of the Cross being made at the words, "Absolution and Remission of all your sins."[^14] If a priest is not present, the Officiant remains kneeling and recites the [Collect](/glossary/liturgical-terms#collect) for the Twenty-First Sunday after Trinity in place of the Absolution.[^15]

❦ The 1928 Prayer Book introduced the shorter alternative Absolution for Evening Prayer, which is drawn from the [Sarum](/liturgy/rites/sarum-rite) Office of Compline.[^16]

## Our Father

All recite the [Our Father](/liturgy/prayers/our-father) kneeling.[^17] It is customary to make the Sign of the Cross at the end of the Our Father.[^18]

❦ The Sarum Breviary states, "Before the beginning of the [Hours](/glossary/liturgical-terms#liturgy-of-the-hours), the Lord's Prayer is always said ... Whence [Jerome](/people/saints/latin-fathers/jerome-of-stridon), 'At the beginning of any work whatsoever, preface it with the Lord's Prayer and the Sign of the Cross on the forehead, according to what is written: Before prayer, prepare thy soul for the Lord; and be not as a man that tempteth God' (Ecclus. 18:23)."[^19]

The Our Father naturally follows the Absolution, for it is through the assurance of forgiveness in Christ that we find the confidence to address God as our heavenly Father. As the burning coal purified Isaiah's lips, so the Absolution cleanses us and prepares us to respond, "Here I am!" (Isa. 6:6-8), to the glory of God's holy Name.

[*The Myroure of Our Ladye*](/meditations/the-lords-prayer-an-exposition-of-the-our-father) wonderfully expounds the rich meaning of the Our Father.[^20]

## Office Proper
---

## Opening Versicles

The Opening Versicles are chanted kneeling.[^21] It is customary to make the Sign of the Cross on the lips with the thumb of the right hand at the words "O Lord, open thou our lips."[^22][^23] All stand and bow toward the altar at the [*Gloria Patri*](/liturgy/music/chants/gloria-patri).[^24] At the words, "As it was in the beginning," all rise and return to their lateral position.

❦ The 6th-century [*Rule of Saint Benedict*](/meditations/the-rule-of-saint-benedict) prescribed two [versicles](/glossary/liturgical-terms#versicle) for the opening of [Matins](/glossary/liturgical-terms#matins): the [*Domine labia mea*](/liturgy/music/chants/domine-labia-mea) and the [*Deus in adjutorium*](/liturgy/music/chants/deus-in-adjutorium). This was to be followed by the *Gloria Patri* and the [*Alleluia*](/liturgy/music/chants/alleluia). The *Domine labia mea* was to be omitted the other hours of the day, and the *Alleluia* was to be omitted during certain times of the year.[^25] The Prayer Book simplified these rubrics by appointing the *Domine labia mea* for both Morning and Evening Prayer—as it flows more naturally into the praise of the *Gloria Patri* and the Invitatory—and by replacing the *Alleluia* with its English equivalent, "Praise ye the Lord," to be used throughout the year. It also added the response, "The Lord's Name be praised," thus allowing the entire opening to be chanted <Definition lang="english" anchor="antiphonal" text="antiphonally" />.[^26]

<small>*<Red text="+" /> Unless seated, it is customary to bow toward the altar whenever reciting the Gloria Patri.*</small><br/>

<small>*<Red text="+" /> This Office concludes every psalm and [canticle](/glossary/liturgical-terms#canticle) with the Gloria Patri,[^27] even when the Prayer Book permits an alternative.*</small><br/>

<small>*<Red text="+" /> According to ancient precedent, the Opening Versicles and the Invitatory are omitted during the last three days of Holy Week and on All Souls' Day in recognition of the solemn character of these observances.[^28]*</small>

## Invitatory

All standing, the [Invitatory](/glossary/liturgical-terms#invitatory) (Psalm 95, the [*Venite*](/liturgy/music/chants/venite)) is chanted at Morning Prayer.[^29] The Prayer Book supplies Invitatory [antiphons](/glossary/liturgical-terms#antiphon) for all the seasons of the liturgical year except for Lent. The Lenten antiphon can be found in the Psalter ([*Saint Dunstan's Psalter*](/glossary/liturgical-terms#saint-dunstans-plainsong-psalter), abbreviated SDP).[^30] For simplicity, this Office commends chanting each antiphon throughout its season rather than exclusively on its appointed feast day. In this way, the *Venite* is always sung with an antiphon, in keeping with the ancient tradition. The Psalter includes two endings for the *Venite*. The first, drawn from Psalm 96:9 and Psalm 96:13, is from the Prayer Book and is the one appointed for the Office. The second comes directly from Psalm 95.

❦ As the name implies, the Invitatory serves as a calling or invitation to praise. The 6th-century *Rule of Saint Benedict* prescribed the *Venite* as the Invitatory for Matins and directed that it be sung with an antiphon.[^31] The exact manner of singing the Invitatory antiphon has varied over the centuries. The 15th-century *Myroure of Our Ladye* explains that it is sung five times in full during the *Venite*, corresponding to the five senses, which those who serve the Lord must carefully guard.[^32] Accordingly, the Psalter marks five places in the *Venite* where the antiphon is recited.

<small>*<Red text="+" /> On days when the Venite is sung, Psalm 95 is to be omitted from the Psalms of the Day.[^33]*</small><br/>

<small>*<Red text="+" /> During the [Octave](/glossary/liturgical-terms#octave) of Easter, the [Pascha nostrum](/liturgy/music/chants/pascha-nostrum) replaces the Venite.[^34]*</small><br/>

<small>*<Red text="+" /> Although the Prayer Book permits the omission of the Venite on Ash Wednesday, this Office retains it according to ancient precedent.[^35][^36]*</small><br/>

<small>*<Red text="+" /> As an optional devotional practice, the original ending for Psalm 95 may be used.*</small>

## Psalms of the Day

All sit for the chanting of the Psalms of the Day.[^37]

❦ Antiphonal psalmody traces its origins to the Jewish temple, and the early Christians continued on in the regular chanting of psalms.[^38] The 6th-century *Rule of Saint Benedict* outlined a seven-day psalter, so that by the end of each week all 150 Psalms would have been chanted—a task, Benedict notes, that his predecessors had accomplished within a single day.[^39] In order to make the Office more accessible to the laity, the Prayer Book adopted a monthly psalter.[^40]

The appointed posture for chanting the psalms is seated, as it is properly a meditation.[^41]

## First Lesson

All sit for the reading of the First Lesson from the Old Testament.[^42] The Reader stands and approaches the [lectern](/glossary/liturgical-terms#lectern). Before reading, he says, "Here beginneth the *nth* verse of the *nth* chapter of the Book of *N*." After reading, he says, "Here endeth the *Nth* Lesson."

<small>*<Red text="+" /> The lessons for the Office follow the Prayer Book's one-year [lectionary](/glossary/liturgical-terms#lectionary) and therefore may not always correspond precisely to the feast or Sunday.[^43] The Epistle and Gospel appointed for the feast or Sunday are read at Holy Communion.*</small>

## Te Deum

All stand for the chanting of the [*Te Deum*](/liturgy/music/chants/te-deum) at Morning Prayer.[^44] The *Te Deum* is traditionally sung on the following occasions:

- **All Feasts and Lord's Days**
- **Daily during:**
  - Christmastide
  - Octave of Epiphany
  - Eastertide, except on [Rogation Days](/liturgy/liturgical-year/seasons/eastertide/rogation-days)
  - Whitsuntide, except on [Ember Days](/glossary/liturgical-terms#ember-days)
- **Omitted during:**
  - Advent
  - The Holy Innocents, unless it falls on a Sunday
  - From Septuagesima to Easter (exclusive)
  - All Souls' Day[^45][^46]

❦ The *Te Deum* is an ancient hymn. Some manuscripts attribute it to [St. Ambrose](/people/saints/latin-fathers/ambrose-of-milan) and [St. Augustine](/people/saints/latin-fathers/augustine-of-hippo), giving rise to the belief that it was composed for Augustine’s baptism in 386 AD. More likely, however, their names became associated with the hymn because they helped introduce it into the Western Church. Scholars generally agree that, in its present form, the hymn was composed around the 4th century.[^47] In the 6th century, it was prescribed for Matins on Lord's Days in the *Rule of Saint Benedict*.[^48]

## Benedicite

All stand for the chanting of the [*Benedicite*](/liturgy/music/chants/benedicite) at Morning Prayer on days when the *Te Deum* is not sung.[^49]

❦ The words of the *Benedicite* come from the *Prayer of Azariah and Song of the Three Holy Children*, which is found in the [Septuagint](/bible/translations/septuagint) version of Daniel 3 and was included in the [Vulgate](/bible/translations/vulgate). The *Benedicite* belongs to the ancient weekly cycle of [Old Testament Canticles](/liturgy/daily-office/psalter#old-testament-canticles) recited at Lauds and was originally appointed for Lord's Days.[^50]

<small>*<Red text="+" /> The Gloria Patri is not recited at the end of the Benedicite, as it ends with its own [doxology](/glossary/liturgical-terms#doxology).[^51]*</small><br/>

<small>*<Red text="+" /> As an optional devotional practice, one may wish to chant the Old Testament Canticle appointed for the day in place of the Te Deum on days when it is not sung.*</small><br/>

<small>*<Red text="+" /> As an optional devotional practice, those comfortable singing in Latin may wish to follow the Benedictine tradition of singing the *Benedicite* on Christmas Day with the antiphon [*Facta est cum Angelo*](/liturgy/music/chants/facta-est-cum-angelo).[^52]*</small>

## Magnificat

All stand for the chanting of the [*Magnificat*](/liturgy/music/chants/magnificat) at Evening Prayer.[^53]

The [O Antiphons](/liturgy/liturgical-year/seasons/advent/o-antiphons) have long accompanied the *Magnificat* at Vespers during the last eight nights of Advent.[^54] Accordingly, this Office uses the verses of the English paraphrase, [*O Come, O Come, Emmanuel*](/liturgy/music/hymns/o-come-o-come-emmanuel), as antiphons for the *Magnificat* on these nights.[^55] Five of the verses are drawn from [*Hymns Ancient & Modern*](/glossary/liturgical-terms#hymns-ancient--modern) (1861).[^56] The verses for [*O Sapientia*](/liturgy/music/chants/o-sapientia) and [*O Rex gentium*](/liturgy/music/chants/o-rex-gentium), which were paraphrased later, are taken from *The Hymnal of the Episcopal Church* (1940).[^57] The paraphrase of [*O Virgo virginum*](/liturgy/music/chants/o-virgo-virginum)—the eighth O Antiphon according to the medieval English use—is unique to this Office. The verses are sung to the traditional melody, the earliest surviving manuscripts of which date back to the 16th century.[^58]

❦ *The Rule of Saint Benedict* prescribed a Gospel Canticle after the Lesson at [Vespers](/glossary/liturgical-terms#vespers).[^59] In 820 AD, Amalarius, Frankish bishop and contemporary of Charlemagne, attested to the use of the *Magnificat* as this canticle.[^60] Accordingly, the Prayer Book places the *Magnificat* after the First Lesson at Evening Prayer. This placement is fitting because, like [Our Lady](/people/saints/biblical/blessed-virgin-mary)—who first proclaimed the words of this canticle—it links together the Old and New Testaments.

<small>*<Red text="+" /> It is customary to make the Sign of the Cross at the beginning of each Gospel Canticle.[^61]*</small><br/>

<small>*<Red text="+" /> Although the Prayer Book permits certain omissions and substitutions, this Office preserves all the Canticles in full at their appointed places.*</small><br/>

<small>*<Red text="+" /> A canticle may be both read and sung in the same Office, provided it is neither read twice nor sung twice.*</small>

## Second Lesson

All sit for the reading of the Second Lesson from the New Testament, which is read in the same manner as the First Lesson.[^62]

## Benedictus

All stand for the chanting of the [*Benedictus*](/liturgy/music/chants/benedictus) at Morning Prayer.[^63]

❦ *The Rule of Saint Benedict* prescribed a Gospel Canticle after the Lessons at Lauds.[^64] In 820 AD, Amalarius attested to the use of the *Benedictus* as this canticle.[^65] Accordingly, the Prayer Book places the *Benedictus* after the Second Lesson at Morning Prayer. This placement is fitting because, like [St. John the Baptist](/people/saints/biblical/john-the-baptist)—about whom this canticle is written—it links together the Old and New Testaments.

## Nunc dimittis

All stand for the chanting of the [*Nunc dimittis*](/liturgy/music/chants/nunc-dimittis) at Evening Prayer.[^66]

❦ The *Nunc dimittis* has been used as an evening prayer since at least the 4th century, when it was included in the *Apostolic Constitutions*.[^67] In 820 AD, Amalarius attested to its use in the Office after the Lesson at Compline.[^68] Accordingly, the Prayer Book places the *Nunc dimittis* after the Second Lesson at Evening Prayer. This placement is fitting because, like Simeon—who, when first reciting this canticle, beheld the face of the child King—we end the day beholding the Lord in his incarnate glory.

<small>*<Red text="+" /> As an optional devotional practice, those comfortable singing in Latin may follow the Benedictine tradition of singing the *Nunc dimittis* on Lord's Days with the antiphon [*Salva nos Domine*](/liturgy/music/chants/salva-nos-domine).[^69]*</small>

## Optional Third Lesson

The Psalter suggests an optional Third Lesson after the Gospel Canticles for the commemoration of saints.[^70] Lessons have been provided for this purpose and are drawn from the 1897 edition of [*Lives of the Saints*](/glossary/liturgical-terms#lives-of-the-saints).[^71] The Third Lesson is read in the same manner as the other Lessons, except the Reader introduces the passage by saying, "Here beginneth a reading on the life of *N*."

## Concluding Rites
---

## Apostles' Creed

All stand and face the altar for the [Apostles' Creed](/liturgy/creeds/apostles-creed), the Officiant standing [*junctis manibus*](/glossary/liturgical-terms#junctis-manibus). It is customary to bow the head at the Name of Jesus, and to make the Sign of the Cross at the end of the Creed.[^72][^73]

❦ The Apostles' Creed is one of the most enduring and widely used Christian confessions of faith, tracing its roots back to as early as the 2nd century. It developed from the Old Roman Creed, reaching its present form by the 8th century.[^74] The recitation of the Apostles' Creed at Morning and Evening Prayer reflects its ancient use at Prime and Compline.[^75]

## Salutation

The choir returns to its lateral position, and the priest or deacon turns to the congregation and recites the [Salutation](/glossary/liturgical-terms#salutation), extending and then joining his hands.[^76][^77] If the Officiant is not a priest or deacon, he recites the following from his lateral position:

<Poetry>
|  \V O Lord, hear our prayer.
|  \R And let our cry come unto thee.
|  \V Let us pray.[^78]
</Poetry>

## Suffrages

Immediately following the Salutation, all kneel for the [Suffrages](/glossary/liturgical-terms#suffrages) and remain kneeling until the end of the Office.[^79] If the Officiant is a priest or deacon, he may continue to stand.[^80]

❦ The Suffrages are intercessions meant to serve as an active response to [St. Paul's](/people/saints/apostles/paul) exhortation from his First Epistle to Timothy: "I urge that supplications, prayers, intercessions, and thanksgivings be made for all people, for kings and all who are in high positions, that we may lead a peaceful and quiet life, godly and dignified in every way. This is good, and it is pleasing in the sight of God our Savior, who desires all people to be saved and to come to the knowledge of the truth" (1 Tim. 2:1-4).

## Collects

The Officiant recites the Collects.[^81] The Collects are chanted either (1) using the [festal tone](/liturgy/daily-office/chant-rubrics#the-collects), or (2) *<Definition lang="latin" anchor="recto-tono" text="recto tono" />* on [ferial](/glossary/liturgical-terms#ferial) days.[^82] The Collects of the Day are chanted first, followed by the Collects belonging to the Ordinary: *For Peace* and *For Grace* at Morning Prayer and *For Peace* and *For Aid Against Perils* at Evening Prayer. The Collects are drawn principally from the Prayer Book.[^83] When the Prayer Book does not provide one, the appointed Collect is drawn from the 1880 edition of [*Breviary Offices*](/glossary/liturgical-terms#breviary-offices).[^84] Collects for the commemoration of saints are drawn from the 2024 edition of [*Lesser Feasts and Fasts*](/glossary/liturgical-terms#lesser-feasts-and-fasts).[^85]

❦ *The Rule of Saint Benedict* prescribed that the Day Hours end with the "dismissals".[^86] The nature of these "dismissals" is unclear, but in 820 AD, Amalarius attested to the use of the Suffrages and Collect at the end of the Office.[^87] The Suffrages and Collect likewise appear at the end of the Office in the ancient Sarum Use.[^88]

## Concluding Prayers

One or more concluding prayers may be used at the discretion of the Minister.[^89]

❦ The 1549 Prayer Book concluded the Office with the Collects.[^90] In 1662, a rubric was introduced to allow for the singing of an <Definition lang="greek" anchor="antiphona" text="Anthem" />, and a series of concluding prayers were also provided, such as *A Prayer for the King’s Majesty*, *A Prayer for the Royal Family*, and *A Prayer for the Clergy and People*.[^91] The 1789 American Prayer Book replaced the prayers for the King and Royal Family with *A Prayer for The President of the United States, and all in Civil Authority*.[^92] The 1928 Prayer Book left the use of these prayers to the discretion of the Minister.

## The Grace

The Officiant recites the Grace (2 Cor. 13:14) to conclude the Office.[^93] It is customary to make the Sign of the Cross at the beginning of the Grace.[^94]

❦ *The Rule of Saint Benedict* prescribed that the hours of Matins and Compline be concluded with a [benediction](/glossary/liturgical-terms#benediction).[^95] The 1662 Prayer Book appointed the Grace—the ancient benediction of St. Paul from his Second Epistle to the Corinthians—for the conclusion of the Office.[^96] As the name implies, the Grace serves as a prayer for “the grace of our Lord Jesus Christ, and the love of God, and the fellowship of the Holy Ghost” (2 Cor. 13:14).

The Office thus begins with repentance, moves through the singing and hearing of God's Word, and ends with a petition for divine favor in the words of the Apostle. The Sign of the Cross is made at the Grace, marking the conclusion of the Office and serving as a visible prayer for God's continuing presence.

## After the Office
---

According to the *Rule of Saint Benedict*, whatever time remains after Morning Prayer is to be devoted to thoughtful study of the Psalms and Lessons.[^97] It is also appropriate to include here the study of hymns, the Church Fathers, and the lives of the saints.
