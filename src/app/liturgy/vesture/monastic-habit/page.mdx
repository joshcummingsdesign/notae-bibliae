The monastic garb. From the Latin *<Definition lang="latin" anchor="habitus" text="habitus" />*, which means "attire".

## Cincture

The rope-like belt encircling the body around the waist. From the Latin *<Definition lang="latin" anchor="cinctura" text="cinctura" />* which means "belt". The cincture represents the virtue of chastity as well as girding one's loins in readiness to serve the Lord (Luke 12:35-36).

## Cowl

The hood. From the Latin *<Definition lang="latin" anchor="cucullus" text="cucullus" />*, which means "hood". The cowl represents the helmet of salvation that protects the monk from imprudence and the attacks of the evil one (Eph. 6:17). It also keeps the monk focused and humble, keeping their gaze on the path to the kingdom of heaven, not turning aside to the right hand or to the left (Deut. 5:32-33).

## Sandals

Monks wear simple, inexpensive sandals that can be locally obtained. Along with sandals, monks also wear shoes for manual labor and in the colder winter months. Like the other parts of the habit, their emphasis is humility and unity, not standing out.

## Scapular

The apron-like garment worn over the tunic which drapes over the shoulders. From the Latin *<Definition lang="latin" anchor="scapula" text="scapula" />*, which means "shoulder". Practically, it helps to keep the tunic clean. Spiritually, it represents taking the yoke of Christ upon oneself to serve him through work and prayer (Matt. 11:29).

## Tunic

The long garment worn over the torso. The black color not only represents the monk's death and mortality as he wisely makes use of his time as he awaits the future glories of heaven, but is a reminder that he has died to the world and now lives a life hidden in Christ (Col. 3:3). Further, he has put off the old man with his deeds, and has put on the new man who is renewed in knowledge according to the image of him who created him (Col. 3:9-10).
