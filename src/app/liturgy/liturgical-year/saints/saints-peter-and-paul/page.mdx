<Initial text="Saints Peter and Paul" />

**June 29**<br/>
Apostles (65–66 AD)<br/>
*Feast*

## Morning Prayer

[[St. Peter](/people/saints/apostles/peter)], the great prince of the apostles, was born at Bethsaida. The particular time of his birth cannot be ascertained, but he was probably at least ten years older than his Divine Master. His original name was Simon. He was the son of a man named Jonas (Matt. 16:17), and was brought up in his father's occupation, a fisherman on the sea of Tiberias. He and his brother [Andrew](/people/saints/apostles/andrew) were partners of Saints [James](/people/saints/apostles/james) and [John](/people/saints/apostles/john), the sons of Zebedee, who had hired servants (Mark 1:20); and from various indications in the sacred narrative, we are led to the conclusion that their social position brought them into contact with men of education. The Apostle did not live as a mere labouring man in a hut by the seaside, but first at Bethsaida, and afterwards in a house at Capernaum belonging to himself or to his mother-in-law. That he was an affectionate husband, married in early life to a wife who accompanied him in his apostolic journeys, are facts inferred from Scripture ... [Ancient tradition informs] us that her name was Perpetua, [and] that she bore him a daughter named Petronilla, and perhaps other children.

St. Peter was probably about the age of thirty-five or forty at the date of his call. That call was preceded by a special preparation. He and his brother Andrew, together with their partners Saints James and John, were disciples of [St. John the Baptist](/people/saints/biblical/john-the-baptist) (John 1:35), and were in attendance upon him when they were first summoned by Christ. This first call, [recorded by St. John], led to no immediate change in St. Peter's external position. He and his fellow disciples do not seem to have immediately followed Christ as his regular disciples. They returned to Capernaum, where they pursued their usual business, waiting for a further intimation of the will of God. [The second call—recorded by Saints [Matthew](/people/saints/apostles/matthew), [Mark](/people/saints/evangelists/mark), and [Luke](/people/saints/evangelists/luke)—took place] on the Sea of Galilee, near Capernaum, where the four disciples (Saints Peter, Andrew, James, and John) were fishing. Saints Peter and Andrew were first called.

Immediately after that call, our Lord went to the house of St. Peter, where he wrought the miracle of healing the step-mother of the Apostle, who was lying sick [with a] fever. St. Peter then followed Christ closely, and was in constant attendance [upon] him in Galilee, Decapolis, Perea and Judaea. The special designation of St. Peter and the rest of the apostles took place some time afterwards (Matt. 10:2-4). They appear then first to have received formally the name of apostles; and from that time, Simon bore publicly—and as it would seem all but exclusively—the name Peter, which had hitherto been used rather as a characteristic [title] than as a proper name. From this time, St. Peter held the chief place among the Twelve. The precedence did not depend on priority of call, [and] it seems hardly probable that it depended on seniority. Some special designation by Christ alone accounts in a satisfactory way for the fact that he is named first in every list of the apostles, is generally addressed by our Lord as their representative, and on the most solemn occasions speaks in their name.

Towards the close of our Lord's ministry, St. Peter's characteristics became especially prominent. At the Last Supper, St. Peter seems to have been particularly earnest in the request that the traitor might be pointed out. After the Supper, his words drew out the meaning of the significant, sacramental act of our Lord in washing his disciples' feet. Then too it was that he made those repeated protestations of unalterable fidelity, so soon to be falsified by his miserable fall. On the morning of the Resurrection, we have proof that St. Peter, though humble, was not crushed by his fall. He and St. John were the first to visit the sepulchre; he was the first who entered it. We are told by St. Luke and by [St. Paul](/people/saints/apostles/paul) that Christ appeared to him first among the apostles. It is observable, however, that on that occasion he is called by his original name Simon, not Peter; the higher designation was not restored until he had been publicly reinstituted ... That reinstitution took place at the Sea of Galilee (John 21:15-18), an event of the very highest [significance]. ... The thrice repeated questions of Christ, referring doubtless to the three protestations and denials, were thrice met by answers full of love and faith. He then received the formal commission to feed Christ's sheep as one who had forfeited his place and could not resume it without such an authorization. Then followed the prediction of his martyrdom in which he was to find the fulfilment of his request to be permitted to follow the Lord.

## Evening Prayer

[St. Peter is] said to have founded the church in Rome about the second year of the emperor Claudius. At his first arrival, he devoted himself chiefly to the Jews—his countrymen—who, ever since the time of Augustus, had dwelt in the region beyond the Tiber. But when afterwards he began to preach to the Gentiles, tradition says that he changed his lodging and was taken into the house of one Pudens, a senator [who had recently] converted to the faith. Here he is said to have met with the celebrated Jew Philo—who had [recently] come on his second [trip] to Rome on behalf of his countrymen at Alexandria—and to have contracted an intimate friendship and acquaintance with him. After St. Peter had been several years at Rome, the emperor Claudius, taking advantage of some seditions and tumults raised by the Jews, by a public edict banished them out of Rome. ... It is related that towards the latter end of the reign of the emperor Nero, St. Peter returned to Rome, where he found the minds of the people strangely bewitched and hardened against embracing Christianity by the subtleties and magical arts of Simon Magus, whom he had formerly confounded at Samaria, when that wretched man sought to purchase the gifts of the Holy Ghost with money, as is related in the Acts of the Apostles.

[Eusebius](/people/scholars/eusebius), in his *Ecclesiastical History*, quotes a writer Caius, born in the time of Zephyrinus, Bishop of Rome (202–218), who says, "I can show [you] the [monuments] of the Apostle. For if you will go to the Vatican or to the Ostian Road, you will find the [monuments] of those who have laid the foundation of this Church." And that both suffered martyrdom about the same time, we learn from Dionysius, Bishop of Corinth (178 AD), who gives the following testimony quoted by Eusebius: "You Romans, by means of this admonition, have [united] the flourishing seed that had been planted by Peter and Paul at Rome and Corinth. For both of these having planted us at Corinth, likewise instructed us; and having in like manner taught in Italy, they suffered martyrdom about the same time." Eusebius adds that St. Peter was crucified and St. Paul was beheaded under the emperor Nero.

The story of the death of St. Peter is, however, related in the apocryphal *Acts of Peter* ... Nero ordered St. Peter and St. Paul to be confined in the Mamertine Prison, where they spent their time in the exercises of religion, and especially in preaching to the prisoners and those who resorted to them; and to baptize [them], St. Peter elicited water from the prison wall by striking it. When the day of their execution approached, the Christians in Rome urged St. Peter most earnestly to escape. He resisted their importunities for long, but at length, moved by their tears, he got over the wall of the prison and fled. But as he approached the gate of the city, he met our Blessed Lord bearing his cross, just entering. "Lord, whither goest thou?" asked the astonished Apostle. "I go to be crucified in Rome afresh." St. Peter was smitten to the heart, and with many tears returned and delivered himself into the hands of his keepers, showing himself thenceforth most ready and cheerful to submit to the divine will. The little church of *<Definition lang="latin" anchor="domine-quo-vadis" text='"Domine Quo Vadis?"' />* is believed to stand on the spot sanctified by this reported mysterious meeting. Before his suffering, St. Peter was doubtless scourged according to the usual custom of the Romans in dealing with criminals condemned to capital punishment. Having saluted his brethren, and especially having bidden his last farewell to St. Paul, he was brought out of the prison and led to the top of the Vatican Mount, the place designed for his execution.

The death to which he was condemned was crucifixion, being of all others accounted the most shameful as well as the most severe and terrible. But he entreated the favour of the officers that he might not be crucified in the ordinary way, but might suffer with his head downwards and his feet up to heaven, affirming that he was unworthy to suffer in the same posture wherein his Lord had suffered before him. "Happy man," says [St. Chrysostom](/people/saints/greek-fathers/john-chrysostom), "to be set in the readiest posture of travelling from earth to heaven." His body, after having been taken down from the cross, is said to have been embalmed by Marcellinus the Presbyter, after the Jewish manner, and was then buried in the Vatican near the Triumphal Way. Over his grave a small church was soon after erected, [and], being destroyed by Heliogabalus, his body was removed to the cemetery in the Appian Way, two miles from Rome, where it remained till the time of Cornelius, Bishop of Rome, who reconveyed it to the Vatican. Here it rested somewhat obscurely until the reign of Constantine, who, out of the great reverence which he had for the Christian religion, caused many churches to be built at Rome, but especially rebuilt and enlarged the Vatican in honour of St. Peter. In doing this, the emperor himself is said to have dug the first [spadefuls] of the foundation, and to have carried from the trench twelve baskets of rubbish with his own hands, in honour of the twelve apostles.[^1]
