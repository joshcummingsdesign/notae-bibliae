<Initial text="Liturgical Year: Preface" />

This calendar is based on the 1928 American *Book of Common Prayer*, received here as a faithful representation of the ancient Western liturgical calendar, rendered accessible to the laity and adapted to the pastoral needs of Christians in the United States. To enrich devotional life throughout the year, the Prayer Book calendar has been supplemented with feasts and commemorations drawn principally from the [Use of Sarum](/liturgy/rites/sarum-rite). Civic observances have been omitted in order to maintain the calendar's liturgical focus.

One might rightly ask whether it is appropriate to supplement the Prayer Book calendar, to which I respond: while fully capable of standing on its own, devout Christians throughout the centuries have supplemented the Prayer Book's observances to preserve the rich tradition handed down to them by their predecessors. In light of this precedent, the question is not whether supplementation is appropriate, but rather which observances are most fitting to include. To answer this question, two guiding principles have been applied: (1) observances must reflect long-standing usage in the Western tradition, and (2) observances must demonstrate continuity with the Prayer Book's liturgical heritage.

To reflect long-standing usage, observances must have persisted into the modern Western tradition, as evidenced by their attestation in a calendar grounded in extensive historical and liturgical scholarship. The [General Roman Calendar](/glossary/liturgical-terms#general-roman-calendar) (1969) was used for this purpose. To ensure that the resulting observances are not exclusively Roman, the Church of England's [*Common Worship*](/glossary/liturgical-terms#common-worship) (2000) was also consulted as a point of comparison.

To demonstrate continuity, observances must also be attested in the liturgical tradition from which the Prayer Book derives. The first source consulted for this purpose was the 1868 edition of A. H. Pearson's English translation of the [Sarum Missal](/liturgy/rites/sarum-rite) (early 16th century). The second was the Surtees Society's 1880 edition of the [York Breviary](/glossary/liturgical-terms#use-of-york) (1493), an important witness to English liturgical usage alongside that of Sarum. The third was a digitized copy of the [Roman Breviary](/liturgy/rites/roman-rite) (1529), made available by the National Library of France, providing a witness within the wider Western tradition.

The dates of observances are maintained as found in these sources. When an observance falls on a day already occupied, it is transferred to the next available day.

The resulting calendar captures the spirit of the ancient Sarum Use, while allowing the Prayer Book's simplicity to shine elegantly in the foreground. Because each observance is well attested, the calendar may be used with the utmost confidence.

It is my most sincere prayer that no unintentional errors were made in compiling this calendar, and that its use builds you up in the faith and draws you more deeply into the Church's continual offering of praise and prayer.

*— J. C. Cummings*
