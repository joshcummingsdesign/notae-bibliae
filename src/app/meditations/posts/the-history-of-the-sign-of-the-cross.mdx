The [Sign of the Cross](/liturgy/prayers/sign-of-the-cross) is a blessing or prayer made with the body. It is a proclamation of the Holy Trinity, an acknowledgement of Christ's life, death, and resurrection, and an aid in spiritual warfare. But how is it made, and how did it come to be?

## Origins

The use of the Sign of the Cross traces back to as early as the 3rd century. [Tertullian](/people/scholars/tertullian) speaks of it in his treatise *De Corona*, stating that it is traced upon the forehead (Ezek. 9:4; Rev. 7:3).

> "At every forward step and movement, at every going in and out, when we put on our clothes and shoes, when we bathe, when we sit at table, when we light the lamps, on couch, on seat, in all the ordinary actions of daily life, we trace upon the forehead the Sign."
>
> <cite>[Tertullian, *De Corona*, ch. 3](https://www.newadvent.org/fathers/0304.htm)</cite>

[St. Hippolytus](/people/saints/early/hippolytus-of-rome) in his *Apostolic Tradition* directs that the Sign be used during Baptism, before praying at fixed prayer times, and in times of temptation.[^1]

In the 4th century, [St. Cyril of Jerusalem](/people/saints/greek-fathers/cyril-of-jerusalem) called it the "dread of devils," saying, "For when they see the Cross they are reminded of the Crucified; they are afraid of him, who bruised the heads of the dragon."[^2] It comes as no surprise, then, that the Sign of the Cross was used in times of persecution. Thus Lactantius writes: "Diocletian, as being of a timorous disposition, was a searcher into futurity, and during his abode in the East he began to slay victims, that from their livers he might obtain a prognostic of events; and while he sacrificed, some attendants of his, who were Christians, stood by, and they put the immortal Sign on their foreheads. At this the demons were chased away, and the holy rites interrupted."[^3]

## The Eastern Cross

In the East, during the [Monophysite](/glossary/historical-terms#monophysitism) controversy in the Early Middle Ages, Nicene Christians began to use two fingers when making the Sign of the Cross to represent the two natures of Christ. Over time, the larger Sign of the Cross developed, and the forefinger, middle finger, and thumb were pinched together to symbolize the three persons of the Trinity, while the ring and little finger were folded down to represent the two natures of Christ.

But there were other variations in hand position. The Christogram (ICXC), for example, was made by extending the forefinger, bending the middle finger, crossing the thumb and ring finger, and bending little finger.[^4] In the famous 6th-century Byzantine icon *<Definition lang="greek" anchor="khristos-pantokrator" text="Christ Pantocrator" />*, Christ is depicted with his thumb and last two fingers touching together, his first two fingers extended, and his middle finger slightly bent, the extended forefinger representing his divinity and the bent middle finger representing his humanity.

One of the earliest witnesses to the right cross—that is, moving from the right to the left—comes from Pope Leo IV in the 9th century.

> "Sign the chalice and the host with a right cross and not with circles or with a varying of the fingers, but with two fingers stretched out and the thumb hidden within them, by which the Trinity is symbolized."
>
> <cite>[Pope Leo IV, *Catholic Encyclopedia*, par. 9](https://www.newadvent.org/cathen/13785a.htm)</cite>

The significance of the right cross is explained by St. Peter of Damascus in the 12th century.

> "The two fingers and single hand with which it is made represent the Lord Jesus Christ crucified, and he is thereby acknowledged to exist in two natures and one hypostasis or person. The use of the right hand betokens his infinite power and the fact that he sits at the right hand of the Father. That the Sign begins with a downward movement from above signifies his descent to us from heaven. Again, the movement of the hand from the right side to the left drives away our enemies and declares that by his invincible power the Lord overcame the devil, who is on the left side (Matt 25:33), dark and lacking strength."
>
> <cite>[St. Peter of Damascus, *A Treasury of Divine Knowledge*, 1.40.7](https://orthodoxchurchfathers.com/fathers/philokalia/st-peter-of-damaskos-book-i-a-treasury-of-divine-knowledge.html)</cite>

## The Western Cross

By the Late Middle Ages, many variations of the Sign of the Cross had developed. The West, perhaps seeking a simple and consistent manner in which to perform it, came to favor a left cross with the fingers joined together and extended to signify Christ's Passion leading to his Ascension into heaven, where he is seated at the right hand of God the Father Almighty. This variation is beautifully articulated in the 15th-century *Myroure of Our Ladye*, written for the Bridgettine Sisters of Syon Abbey in West London.

> "In making this Sign of the Cross, you begin with your hand at your head, then move downward, next to the left side, and finally to the right side. This is a sign and a belief that our Lord Jesus Christ came down from the Head, that is, from the Father, to earth through his holy Incarnation. From the earth, he went to the left side, that is, to hell, through his bitter Passion, and from there to his Father's right side through his glorious Ascension. After this, you bring your hand to your chest as a sign that you have come to thank him and praise him with all the love in your heart for these blessings."
>
> <cite>[*The Myroure of Our Ladye*, p. 80](https://archive.org/details/themyroureofoure00unkwuoft/page/n153/mode/2up)</cite>

During the Reformation, the use of the Sign of the Cross with the [Trinitarian Formula](/glossary/liturgical-terms#trinitarian-formula) was further codified. Martin Luther's 1529 *Small Catechism* assigns it to morning and evening prayer, stating: "Bless yourself with the Holy Cross and say, 'In the Name of God the Father, Son, and Holy Ghost. Amen.'"[^5] The Confirmation rites of the 1549 *Book of Common Prayer* direct the priest to say, "I sign thee with the Sign of the Cross, and lay my hand upon thee. In the Name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Ghost. Amen."[^6]

Perhaps the clearest rubric for the Western Cross comes from the 1570 Roman Missal.

> "The celebrant, standing at the lowest step of the altar ... making the Sign of the Cross with his right hand from the forehead to the breast, shall say in an audible voice, 'In the Name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Spirit. Amen.' ... When he signs himself, he places his left hand below his breast. In other blessings, when he is at the altar and is not blessing himself but the offerings or something else, he places it upon the altar. In blessing himself, he turns the palm of his right hand toward himself and, with all its fingers joined together and extended, makes the Sign of the Cross from the forehead to the breast, and from the left shoulder to the right. But if he blesses others, or anything else, he turns the little-finger side of the hand toward the one or thing he blesses and, in giving the blessing, extends his whole right hand with all its fingers likewise joined together and extended. This is to be observed in every blessing."
>
> <cite>[*"De principio Missae"*, *Missale Romanum*, pp. 8–9](https://archive.org/details/ita-bnc-ald-00000255-001/page/8/mode/2up?q=Confessione)</cite>

## Conclusion

The Sign of the Cross is deeply rooted in the history and tradition of the Church. It is rich with symbolism and meaning, and helps to aid us in spiritual warfare. While there are many variations, one thing remains clear: it is a symbol of Christ and our faith, and should be done with the utmost reverence.
