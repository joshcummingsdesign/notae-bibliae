## History

In 530 AD, [St. Benedict](/people/saints/medieval/benedict-of-nursia) founded the great monastery of Monte Cassino, which lies on a hilltop between Rome and Naples. It was around this time that Benedict wrote his famous *Rule*, which he wrote for monks living communally under the authority of an abbot. Benedict was likely influenced by the [*Rule of St. Pachomius*](/people/saints/desert-fathers/pachomius-the-great), the [*Rule of Saint Augustine*](/people/saints/latin-fathers/augustine-of-hippo), and the writings of [St. John Cassian](/people/saints/early/john-cassian). In chapter 73 of his *Rule*, Benedict commends the [*Rule of Saint Basil*](/people/saints/cappadocian-fathers/basil-the-great). The *Rule of Saint Benedict* also shows a strong affinity with the anonymous *Rule of the Master*, which many scholars consider to have preceded Benedict’s *Rule*.

When Monte Cassino was sacked by the Lombards (c. 580), the monks fled to Rome, taking a copy of the *Rule* with them. In 787 this copy was presented to Charlemagne and became the exemplar kept at his palace in Aachen. This exemplar underlies what is now called the "Authentic Text". The earliest extant manuscript of the Authentic Text is the *<Definition lang="latin" anchor="sangallensis" text="Sangallensis" />* (c. 820). The Authentic Text had little appeal in the transalpine regions, as its use of Vulgar Latin did not suit contemporary literary habits. Revised forms of the *Rule* were already in circulation by the 7th century, the earliest extant manuscript being the *<Definition lang="latin" anchor="oxoniensis" text="Oxoniensis" />* (c. 700). By the 10th century, a harmonization of the Authentic and Revised texts had developed, and it endured for many centuries. This version is referred to as the *<Definition lang="latin" anchor="textus-receptus" text='"Textus Receptus"' />*, or more commonly, the “Vulgate Text”.

A notable modern edition of the Vulgate Text, *<Definition lang="latin" anchor="sancti-benedicti-regula-monasteriorum" text="S. Benedicti Regula Monasteriorum" />*, was published in 1928 by the German Benedictine monk Dom Benno Linderbauer of Metten Abbey. In 1952, Justin McCann, Abbot of Ampleforth, published a bilingual edition of the *Rule* in Latin and English. In this edition, the Latin text differs from the Vulgate Text only in improved orthography and in the clarification of obscure passages, the latter done in light of the alternative readings provided by the scribes of the Authentic Text.

## Content

The *Rule* consists of a twofold wisdom: spiritual (how to live a Christ-centered life on earth) and administrative (how to run a monastery efficiently). More than half of the chapters describe how to be obedient and humble, and what to do when a member of the community is not. About one-fourth is on the [Liturgy of the Hours](/glossary/liturgical-terms#liturgy-of-the-hours), also known as the <Definition lang="latin" anchor="officium" text="Divine Office" /> or *<Definition lang="latin" anchor="opus-dei" text="Opus Dei" />*, and one-tenth outlines how, and by whom, the monastery should be managed.

The spirit of the *Rule of Saint Benedict* is summarized by *<Definition lang="latin" anchor="pax" text="Pax" />* and *<Definition lang="latin" anchor="ora-et-labora" text="Ora et labora" />*. Compared to other precepts, the *Rule* provides a moderate path between individual zeal and formulaic institutionalism; because of this middle ground, it has been widely popular. Benedict's concerns were his views of the needs of monks in a community environment: namely, to establish due order, to foster an understanding of the relational nature of human beings, and to provide a spiritual father to support and strengthen the individual's ascetic effort and the spiritual growth that is required for the fulfillment of the human vocation, <Definition lang="greek" anchor="theosis" text="theosis" />.

One of Benedict's main principles of the religious life was the renunciation of one's own will and arming oneself with the strong and noble weapons of obedience under the banner of the true King, Christ the Lord. Among these "weapons" were humility, which he divided into 12 key points:

1. Fear God
2. Subordinate one's will to the will of God
3. Be obedient to one's superior
4. Be patient amid hardships
5. Confess one's sins
6. Hold oneself as a humble servant
7. Consider oneself inferior to all
8. Follow examples set by superiors
9. Do not speak until spoken to
10. Do not readily laugh
11. Speak simply and modestly
12. Express one's inward humility through bodily posture

*The Rule of Saint Benedict*—drawing from the [Apostolic Fathers](/people/saints/apostolic-fathers), [Desert Fathers](/people/saints/desert-fathers), and St. John Cassian—gives a detailed description of the Divine Office as seven daytime hours and one at night, which are associated with Psalm 119:164, "Seven times a day I praise you," and Psalm 119:62, "At midnight I rise to praise you."

## Conclusion

*The Rule of St. Benedict* has guided monastic life for over 15 centuries, and remains among the most enduring expressions of Western monasticism. Its abiding influence lies in the rare balance it maintains between spiritual depth and sober order, directing the whole course of life toward God through obedience, humility, and the continual offering of praise and prayer.
