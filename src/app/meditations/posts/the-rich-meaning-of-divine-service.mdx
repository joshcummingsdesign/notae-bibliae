The rich meaning of Divine Service is something that would take a lifetime to expound; yet from time to time we are given a glimpse worth reflecting upon. The introduction to the 1880 edition of the English [diurnal](/glossary/liturgical-terms#diurnal), [*Breviary Offices*](/glossary/liturgical-terms#breviary-offices), concludes with the following:

> "Space does not allow us here to dwell on the spiritual meaning which underlies the whole Office, and so richly repays investigation."
>
> <cite>[Society of St. Margaret, *Breviary Offices*, xxi](https://archive.org/details/breviaryofficesf00cath/page/n23/mode/2up)</cite>

Then it quotes a most wonderful passage from the 15th-century *Myroure of Our Ladye*, written for the Bridgettine Sisters of Syon Abbey in West London.

> "The changing from one thing to another in Divine Service is ordained to drive away your dullness, that you should not wax tedious and weary, but should gladly and joyfully (not in vain joy, but in joy of spiritual devotion) continue in the service of God. Therefore sometimes you sing, sometimes you read, sometimes you hear; now one alone, now two together, now all. Sometimes you sit, sometimes you stand, sometimes you bow, sometimes you kneel; now toward the altar, now toward the choir, now in stalls, now in the midst. And in all this, you mingle hymns with psalms, and psalms with antiphons, and antiphons with versicles, and lessons with responses, and responses with verses, and so forth in many like ways. And all to the praising of our Lord Jesus Christ ...; and so to exercise the body for the quickening of the soul, that thereby all such bodily observances should not be without spiritual understanding. Now join to all this the fruit of that which is sung and read; and thereto the fellowship of the angels amongst you in time of Divine Service; and most of all, the marvelous and unspeakable presence of God himself ...; and consider whether it be not, as it were, another heaven to serve and praise God in the choir."
>
> <cite>[Sisters of Syon, *The Myroure of Our Ladye*, p. 96–77](https://archive.org/details/themyroureofoure00unkwuoft/page/n169/mode/2up)</cite>
