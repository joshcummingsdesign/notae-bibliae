When something proceeds from a principle of the same nature, both the one proceeding and the source of procession have real, not merely logical, relations to one another ([ST I, q. 28, a. 1, co.](https://aquinas.cc/la/en/~ST.I.Q28.A1.C)).

For relations to be real and not merely logical, there must be a real distinction between the things related. A human father and a son, for example, have real relations and can be distinguished because they are two distinct beings. The Persons of the Trinity have real relations, but they share the same essence and are one in being. Thus, they can be distinguished only by the relations they have to one another ([ST I, q. 28, a. 3, co.](https://aquinas.cc/la/en/~ST.I.Q28.A3.C)).

The Father is distinct because he begets the Son. The Son is distinct because he is begotten of the Father. If we were to say that the Holy Spirit proceeds from the Father alone, there would be no real distinction between him and the Son. Therefore, we must say that the Holy Spirit proceeds from both the Father and the Son. ([ST I, q. 36, a. 2, co., pars. 1–3](https://aquinas.cc/la/en/~ST.I.Q36.A2.C)).
