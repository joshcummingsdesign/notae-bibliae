**Does the Holy Spirit proceed from the Father and the Son (*<Definition lang="latin" anchor="filioque" text="Filioque" />*)?**

A relation of origin is a relation based on one thing proceeding from another as its source. When two things in a relation of origin share the same nature, their relation is real, not merely logical. A father and son, for example, have real relations because they have a relation of origin—the father begets the son—and they share the same human nature. The [Nicene Creed](/liturgy/creeds/niceno-constantinopolitan-creed) tells us that the Persons of the Trinity have a relation of origin—the Son is begotten of the Father and the Holy Spirit proceeds from the Father—and that they share the same divine nature. Thus, they have real, not merely logical, relations. For if their relations were merely logical, we would fall into [Sabellianism](/glossary/historical-terms#sabellianism) ([ST I, q. 28, a. 1, s.c.](https://aquinas.cc/la/en/~ST.I.Q28.A1.SC)).

Further, if there were no real distinction between the Persons, their relations would be merely logical, and we would again fall into Sabellianism. We know a father and son, for example, have real relations because they are two distinct beings. The Persons of the Trinity, however, share the same essence and are one in being, thus they can only be distinguished from one another by their relations of origin ([ST I, q. 28, a. 3, co.](https://aquinas.cc/la/en/~ST.I.Q28.A3.C)).

We know that the Father is a distinct Person because he begets the Son. We know that the Son is a distinct Person because he is begotten of the Father. But if we were to say that the Holy Spirit proceeds only from the Father, there would be no real distinction between the Son and the Holy Spirit. Therefore, we must say that the Holy Spirit proceeds from both the Father and the Son. ([ST I, q. 36, a. 2, co., pars. 1–3](https://aquinas.cc/la/en/~ST.I.Q36.A2.C)).
