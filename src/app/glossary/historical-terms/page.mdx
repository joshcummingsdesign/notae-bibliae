## Arianism

A heretical view which holds that Jesus Christ is not coeternal with the Father, but was created by God before anything else. Named after its proponent Arius, a priest in Alexandria.

## Council of Nicaea

The first ecumenical council of the Church in 325 AD, in which the [Nicene Creed](/liturgy/creeds/nicene-creed)—Christianity's defining statement of belief—was drawn up. The term "Nicene Faith" corresponds to this council, and namely its affirmation of the Holy Trinity.

## Origenism

A set of heretical teachings attributed to [Origen of Alexandria](/people/scholars/origen), including the pre-existence of souls, universalism, and subordinationism.

## Subordinationism

The heretical view that the Son and/or the Holy Spirit are inferior to the Father in essence, divinity, or rank within the Trinity, rather than fully equal and co-eternal with Him, a view condemned at the First Council of Nicaea.

## Universalism

The heretical view that all souls will be saved and reconciled to God, so that no one will suffer eternal damnation.
