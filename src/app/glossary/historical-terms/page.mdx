<Initial text="Historical Terms" />

## Anchorite

<Definition lang="greek" anchor="anakhoreo" text="Anchoritic" /> monasticism is a monastic tradition that stresses withdrawal from secular society. Monks in this tradition are called anchorites. They are considered to be a type of hermit, and the terms "anchorite" and "hermit" are sometime used interchangeably.

## Arianism

A heretical view which holds that Jesus Christ is not coeternal with the Father, but was created by God before anything else. Named after its proponent Arius, a priest in Alexandria. It was condemned at the Council of Nicaea in 325 AD.

## Cenobite

<Definition lang="greek" anchor="koinobion" text="Cenobitic" /> monasticism is a monastic tradition that stresses community life. Monks in this tradition are called cenobites. They live in monasteries, serving under a rule and an <Definition lang="latin" anchor="abbas" text="abbot" />.

## Eremite (Hermit)

<Definition lang="greek" anchor="eremos" text="Eremitic" /> monasticism is a monastic tradition that stresses withdrawal from secular society. Monks in this tradition are called eremites or hermits. According to [St. Benedict](/people/saints/medieval/benedict-of-nursia), they go out well-armed from the ranks of the community to the solitary combat of the desert.

## Gyrovague

<Definition lang="latin" anchor="gyrovagus" text="Gyrovagues" /> were monks who wandered from place to place without stable residence or obedience to a rule, living under their own direction rather than under recognized monastic authority. According to [St. Benedict](/people/saints/medieval/benedict-of-nursia) they were given up to their own wills and the allurements of gluttony.

## Mendicant Orders

<Definition lang="latin" anchor="mendicans" text="Medicant Orders" /> refer to religious orders that have vowed for their male members a lifestyle of poverty, traveling, and living in urban areas for purposes of preaching, evangelization, and ministry, especially to less wealthy individuals. This includes the [Franciscans](/people/saints/medieval/francis-of-assisi) and the [Dominicans](/people/saints/medieval/dominic-of-guzman).

## Nestorianism

The heretical view that Christ exists as two separate persons—one divine and one human—rather than one unified person with both natures. Named after its proponent Nestorius, Patriarch of Constantinople. It was condemned at the Council of Ephesus in 431 AD.

## Nicene

The faith declared at the Council of Nicaea, the first ecumenical council of the Church in 325 AD, in which the [Nicene Creed](/liturgy/creeds/nicene-creed)—Christianity's defining statement of belief—was drawn up. The term "Nicene Faith" corresponds to this council, and namely its affirmation of the Holy Trinity.

## Origenism

A set of heretical teachings attributed to [Origen of Alexandria](/people/scholars/origen), including the pre-existence of souls, universalism, and subordinationism. They were condemned in the 6th century.

## Sabellianism

The heretical view that there is only one Person in the <Definition lang="english" anchor="godhead" text="Godhead" />. Named after its proponent Sabellius, a priest in Rome. It was condemned in the 3th century.

## Sarabaite

A name of Coptic origin which designates a monastic tradition that acknowledged no monastic superior nor obeyed a definite rule. According to [St. Benedict](/people/saints/medieval/benedict-of-nursia), their law was their own good pleasure, and what they liked not they regarded as unlawful.

## Subordinationism

The heretical view that the Son and/or the Holy Spirit are inferior to the Father in essence, divinity, or preeminence within the Trinity, rather than fully equal and co-eternal with him. It was condemned at the Council of Nicaea in 325 AD.

## Universalism

The heretical view that all souls will be saved and reconciled to God, so that no one will suffer eternal damnation. It was condemned in the 6th century.
